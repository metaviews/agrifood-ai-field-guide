## 1. Why this scan now

The user's "track the money" framing is the right heuristic for Europe. Unlike the US (where commercial vendor-led deployment dominates) and unlike China (where state-led deployment dominates), **Europe's agritech AI activity is funded through a multi-layered institutional architecture** — EU programmes, member-state CAP Strategic Plans, industry associations, and farmer-cooperative federations. None of these has a standalone corpus unit. The corpus has Europe-region units for vendors (xFarm, Naïo, Ekonoke, INRAE, CLAAS) but **zero units for the institutions that fund the substrate those vendors operate within**.

This matters because:
- **Where money flows tells you where activity is.** EIT Food's 65-startup 2026 cohort, Horizon Europe Cluster 6's €-billion work programmes, EU Mission Soil's Living Lab network — these define the EU's "in scope" activity zones.
- **What the institutions call "in scope" tells you what counts.** EIT Food's accelerator hub network (six countries) and Horizon Europe's strategic priorities define the de facto European agritech AI scope.
- **The institutional substrate explains structural patterns.** Why is the Netherlands strong on data cooperatives (JoinData)? Why is France strong on robotics (Naïo)? Why is Spain strong on cooperative-AI-cluster pattern? The funder substrate answers these.

## 2. EIT Food — the EU Knowledge and Innovation Community for food systems

### 2.1 What it is

EIT Food is one of nine Knowledge and Innovation Communities (KICs) under the European Institute of Innovation and Technology (EIT), an EU body. EIT Food is the food-systems KIC, headquartered in Leuven, Belgium. It is **funded by the EU (Horizon Europe), member states, and corporate partners**, with corporate sponsorship from Bayer, Cargill, Danone, Mars, and Mondelēz International (per Food Ingredients First, 30 June 2026).

EIT Food is the **most direct EU institutional funder for European agritech AI startups**, with a dedicated accelerator programme, regional hubs across six countries, and corporate-partner integration.

### 2.2 EIT Food Accelerator Network (FAN)

The Accelerator Network is EIT Food's flagship startup programme. **2026 cohort**: 65 startups selected. Per Food Ingredients First (30 June 2026):

> *"EIT Food selects 65 start-ups for its 2026 Food Accelerator Network, supporting innovations across biotech, digital farming, circular food systems, and low-carbon supply chains."*

> *"The latest cohort of 65 start-ups selected for the EIT Food Accelerator Network reflects the breadth of innovation currently emerging across Europe, spanning biotech ingredients, digital and autonomous farming systems, circular food solutions, and low-carbon supply chains."*

**2025 cohort**: also 65 startups (per EU Funding Portal 2025 announcement: *"65 groundbreaking startups to power Europe's agrifoodtech transformation"*).

**Programme structure**:
- Each startup receives a **€3,000 grant** per the EIT Food Accelerator Network programme page.
- Specialized hubs across **six countries** (per Food Ingredients First 2026 article).
- Direct engagement with major industry players (Bayer, Cargill, Danone, Mars, Mondelēz International).
- Tech validation, mentorship, tailored programming, workshops.
- Cohort spans: biotech ingredients, digital and autonomous farming systems, circular food solutions, low-carbon supply chains.

### 2.3 EIT Food grant funding scale

Per EU Funding Portal: *"EIT Food intends to fund commercial projects with a maximum of EUR 400,000 in grant funding per project for a period of up to ten months, with the goal of commercialising innovative products and services."*

Per EIT Food news: *"EIT Food announces €875,000 to accelerate commercialisation of agrifood products and services in Europe."* Awarded to multiple startups including afreshed (Austria) awarded €248,892.

Per LinkedIn / Next Bite Brussels (EIT Food's flagship event): *"€600,000 in total funding was awarded, to startups shaping the future of AgriFoodTech across Europe."*

### 2.4 What EIT Food tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Digital and autonomous farming systems** is a named 2026 cohort vertical. AI-based farming systems (precision agriculture, autonomous robots, computer vision) are explicitly named.
- **Six-country hub network** is the geographic concentration: likely Germany, France, Spain, Netherlands, Italy, and one additional. Worth surfacing as the EU's de facto "scope" for accelerated agritech AI activity.
- **Corporate partners (Bayer, Cargill, Danone, Mars, Mondelēz)** are the bridge from EU institutional funding to corporate deployment. The Bayer × EIT Food connection parallels Bayer's Climate FieldView + Microsoft Azure work (`units/microsoft-azure-data-manager-agriculture.md`); the Cargill × EIT Food connection parallels Cargill's CarVe work (`units/cargill-carve-meat-processing.md`); the Danone × EIT Food connection parallels Danone's regenerative agriculture footprint.
- **65 startups per year** at a small-grant level (€3,000 grant per startup accelerator entry + up to €400,000 commercial-project grant per project) is a modest scale by global venture-funding standards, but with EU institutional backing and corporate-partner follow-on pathways, it functions as **early-stage pipeline rather than scale capital**.

### 2.5 The EIT Food gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on EIT Food. Substantive unit candidate.
- EIT Food's named 2025-2026 cohort startups are not enumerated in the corpus. Mapping the named startups by AI-focus (precision agriculture, autonomous farming, biotech ingredients, circular food, low-carbon supply) would fill a substantive gap.
- The corporate-partner follow-on path (which EIT Food startups land at Bayer / Cargill / Danone / Mars / Mondelēz) is thin in the corpus.

## 3. Horizon Europe — Cluster 6 (Food, Bioeconomy, Natural Resources, Agriculture and Environment)

### 3.1 What it is

Horizon Europe is the EU's 2021-2027 research and innovation framework programme, with a budget of approximately **€95.5 billion** (current prices) over the seven-year period. **Cluster 6** is the food, bioeconomy, natural resources, agriculture and environment pillar.

Per European Commission research-and-innovation site: *"Funding opportunities under Horizon Europe are set out in multiannual work programmes, which cover the large majority of support available."* Cluster 6 work programmes are issued in two-year tranches — the current **2026-2027 work programme** was published December 2025.

### 3.2 Cluster 6 2026-2027 — substantive priorities

Per accelopment.com (analysing the draft Cluster 6 work programme 2026-2027):

- **Cluster 6 destinations** include: Biodiversity and ecosystem health; Sustainable food systems; Circular economy and bioeconomy; Clean environment and zero pollution; Land, ocean and water for climate; Resilient, inclusive, healthy and productive rural, coastal and urban areas; Innovative governance, environmental observations and digital solutions.
- **Single project budgets up to €38 million** for the largest projects in 2026-27 (per Innovate UK Business Connect).
- **Multiannual structure** with strategic priorities set by the European Commission.

### 3.3 Horizon Europe and agritech AI

Cluster 6 work programmes explicitly fund agritech AI through:
- **Destinations on sustainable food systems** — AI for sustainable agriculture, food processing, supply chain.
- **Destinations on innovative governance and digital solutions** — digital twins, IoT, AI for environmental monitoring.
- **EU Mission Soil funding** (separate budget line) — soil monitoring with AI components.
- **EU partnerships** (co-funded programmes) — partnership on agriculture of data, partnership on agroecology.

### 3.4 What Horizon Europe Cluster 6 tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Large single-project grants (up to €38M)** are at a different scale than EIT Food's small-grant accelerator model. Cluster 6 is for **research-and-deployment at scale**; EIT Food is for **early-stage pipeline**.
- **Two-year work programme cadence** defines de facto EU priorities. The 2026-2027 work programme is the current operative document; the 2028-2029 work programme is in drafting.
- **Strategic priorities in 2026-2027** are: sustainable food systems, biodiversity, circular economy, climate adaptation, rural resilience. AI is positioned as an enabler across all of these, not as a vertical of its own.
- **Co-funded partnerships** (e.g., agroecology, agriculture of data) bring EU funding together with member-state and private-sector funding. These are the **bodies that translate Horizon Europe Cluster 6 into deployed European agritech AI activity**.

### 3.5 The Horizon Europe gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on Horizon Europe Cluster 6. Substantive unit candidate.
- **Specific 2026-2027 Cluster 6 AI-relevant calls** are not enumerated in the corpus. Identifying the named calls (topic IDs, budgets, deadlines, awarded projects) is next-cycle work.
- **Horizon Europe Cluster 6 ↔ EIT Food pipeline** is structurally significant but thin in the corpus: how many EIT Food startups graduate into Horizon Europe Cluster 6 funding? This pipeline is the bridge from EU early-stage accelerator funding to EU large-scale research-and-deployment funding.

## 4. EU Mission: A Soil Deal for Europe

### 4.1 What it is

EU Mission: A Soil Deal for Europe is one of five EU Missions under Horizon Europe (alongside Cancer, Climate-Neutral and Smart Cities, Ocean and Waters, Adaptation to Climate Change). Launched 2021. Mission goal: **100 Living Labs and Lighthouses deployed across Europe by 2030** for soil health.

### 4.2 Mission Soil 2025 funding call

Per soill2030.eu (the Mission Soil support project, 15 May 2025): the European Commission launched two new Mission Soil topics focused on Living Labs as part of the Horizon Europe Work Programme 2025.

**Call 1: Living Labs for Soil Remediation and Green Redevelopment of Brownfield (HORIZON-MISS-2025-05-SOIL-01)**
- Single-stage submission
- Expected EU contribution per project: **€12 million**
- Indicative budget: €12 million
- Deadline: 30 September 2025, 17:00 (Brussels time)

**Call 2: Living Labs to Enhance Soil Health in Continental, Boreal and Alpine Regions (HORIZON-MISS-2025-05-SOIL-01-two-stage)**
- Two-stage submission
- Expected EU contribution per project: €12 million
- Indicative budget: **€36 million**
- First stage deadline: 4 September 2025; Second stage: 18 February 2026
- Per project: deployment of 4-5 Living Labs across Continental, Boreal, Alpine biogeographical regions
- Each proposal focused on one biogeographical region; Living Labs established across at least three EU Member States

Both topics are **Research and Innovation Actions**, providing **100% EU funding** and requiring a **multi-actor approach** including support to third parties.

### 4.3 The SOILL support project

The EU funds a dedicated support project for Mission Soil — the SOILL Support Office (HUB). Per soill2030.eu:
- Help Desk launched 5 June 2025 (World Environment Day)
- Online Matchmaking Platform for partnership identification
- Promotional materials and factsheets
- Engagement events across European cities (Brussels, Turin, etc.)

### 4.4 What Mission Soil tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Living Labs as deployment model** — the EU's preferred deployment architecture for soil-health AI is the Living Lab (real-world co-design and testing sites). This is structurally distinct from US vendor-led commercial deployment or China state-led national deployment.
- **Multi-actor approach** — each Living Lab requires farmers, researchers, businesses, and civil society. The multi-actor requirement is the EU's structural commitment to **non-vendor-led deployment**.
- **€48 million for Living Labs in 2025 alone** (€12M + €36M) — substantive scale for a single programme year.
- **100 Living Labs by 2030** — the cumulative target. At current pace, the Mission will fall short of the 100-Living-Lab goal, but the deployment pattern is established.

### 4.5 The Mission Soil gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on Mission Soil. Substantive unit candidate.
- **AI components within Living Labs** are not separately enumerated. The 100 Living Labs presumably include AI/ML components (soil monitoring, ML-based soil classification, satellite imagery analysis) but the AI-specific deployment is thin in publicly available documentation.
- **Living Labs → commercial deployment** transition is the structural question. Living Labs produce pilots; commercial deployment requires vendor or farmer-cooperative uptake. This pipeline is thin in the corpus.

## 5. Copa-Cogeca — the EU farmer and cooperative federation

### 5.1 What it is

Copa-Cogeca is the united voice of EU farmers and agricultural cooperatives. **Represents over 22 million European farmers** (per Copa-Cogeca website). Headquartered in Brussels. Founded 1959 (Copa) and 1959 (Cogeca), merged in 1962.

Copa represents farmers individually; Cogeca represents farmer cooperatives. Together they are the **primary institutional voice for European farmers and farmer-cooperatives in EU policy discussions**.

### 5.2 EU Projects portfolio (per copa-cogeca.eu/projects)

Copa-Cogeca participates as a partner in 15+ named EU-funded projects spanning agriculture, environment, and rural development. Selected projects (per Copa-Cogeca projects page):

- **PREPSOIL** (Jul 2022 – Jun 2025): Mission Soil implementation support; awareness and knowledge on soil needs; co-creation of tools for soil-health Living Labs.
- **QuantiFarm** (Jul 2022 – Dec 2026): *"The QuantiFarm project will support the increased deployment of digital technologies in agriculture. Using results on the use of data-driven technology innovations, the project aims to improve the sustainability of the agri-food value chain, in coordination with the most appropriate behaviour and business model innovations that are necessary for maximising their impact."* EU-wide.
- **EU-FARMBOOK** (Aug 2022 – Jul 2029): *"Support knowledge exchange between all EU and national AKIS actors by further developing, expanding, exploiting and maintaining an easily accessible and user-friendly EU-wide digital platform for practitioners in agriculture, forestry and other rural sectors."*
- **EU4Advice** (Oct 2022 – Sep 2027): connected to EU-FARMBOOK and AKIS (Agricultural Knowledge and Innovation Systems).
- **I-RESTART** (Sep 2022 – Aug 2026): *"Reskilling and upskilling the workforce in the agrifood and veterinary sectors, through retraining the employees leaving the heavy industry, and to hire them in the agrifood sector."*
- **FOODCoST** (Jun 2022 – May 2026): *"Develop tools, policies and business models for pathways towards sustainable production and consumption in the food system through internalising positive and negative externalities."*
- **Safe Habitus** (Jan 2023 – Dec 2027): *"Strengthen Farm Health and Safety Knowledge Innovation Systems and support the EU transition to social sustainability in farming."*
- **Grass Ceiling** (Jan 2023 – Dec 2025): gender equality in rural and agricultural innovation systems.
- **Social Dialogue for Skills** (Sep 2023 – Sep 2025): Agrifood Pact for Skills.
- **Smartchain** (Sep 2019 – Aug 2021): short food supply chains.
- **Innoseta** (May 2018 – Oct 2021): spraying equipment, training, advisory services.
- **GAEA** (Jul 2022 – Jun 2024): entrepreneurship education for women in rural areas.
- **EU SPACE partnership with European Space Agency** (per socialeconomynews.eu): *"Copa-Cogeca and the European Space Agency launch partnership to secure the future of sustainable and digital agriculture."*

### 5.3 What Copa-Cogeca tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **22 million European farmers** represented through national member associations. Copa-Cogeca is the structural way that EU policy reaches European farmers — and the structural way European farmer positions reach EU policy.
- **EU-FARMBOOK and EU4Advice** are the EU's primary knowledge-exchange platforms for agriculture and rural sectors. These are digital platforms with AI components (knowledge graph, semantic search, recommendation systems).
- **Copa-Cogeca × European Space Agency partnership** positions Earth observation (Copernicus, Galileo) data as a critical EU asset for agritech AI. The partnership extends the EU's space-based data infrastructure into the agricultural cooperative domain.
- **QuantiFarm** is the corpus's most direct named EU project on digital-technology deployment in agriculture (digital technologies including AI) at EU-wide scale.

### 5.4 The Copa-Cogeca gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on Copa-Cogeca. Substantive unit candidate.
- **Copa-Cogeca's specific AI position** (position papers, policy submissions to EU AI Act consultations, AI Office consultations) is not surfaced in the corpus. The AI Office and EU AI Act consultations received positions from Copa-Cogeca and other European farmer bodies; these are the documents where European farmer-AI positions are formalised.
- **EU-FARMBOOK deployment-scale outcomes** is a named gap (G-164 in the Spain-COOVAP unit) but the corpus doesn't have a Copa-Cogeca unit to anchor it.

## 6. CEMA — the European Agricultural Machinery Association

### 6.1 What it is

CEMA is the European association representing the agricultural machinery industry. Founded 1959. Headquartered at Avenue de Tervueren 168, 1150 Brussels, Belgium. Member national associations include VDMA (Germany), AXEMA (France), FederUnacoma (Italy), etc.

CEMA represents the **EU agricultural equipment-maker cluster**: CLAAS (Germany), AGCO/Fendt/Massey Ferguson/Valtra (US-HQ but strong European operations), Pöttinger (Austria), Ploeger (Netherlands), Lely (Netherlands), DeLaval (Sweden), SDF (Italy), Kuhn (France), Kverneland (Norway), and others.

### 6.2 CEMA's AI Project Team and 2025 statement

CEMA has a dedicated **Project Team on Artificial Intelligence**. Chair: **Francesca Hennig-Possenti**. Per CEMA publication (13 May 2025), the AI Project Team authored the corpus's most substantive industry-association AI position statement:

> *"The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has long been a beacon of global cooperation for food security, agricultural development, and sustainability. Today, as we face unprecedented environmental and technological change, artificial intelligence (AI) stands out as a transformative force reshaping agriculture... Agricultural machinery manufacturers have embraced AI, striving to balance productivity, environmental protection, and sustainability. Yet AI is not a single product but a collection of different techniques — it requires a complete ecosystem: robust infrastructure, machines and sensors, consistent testing practices, quality data, context knowledge and AI output interpretation skills, as well as real-world validation."*

> *"AI is not a silver bullet, but it is a powerful enabler of equity, resilience, and innovation if accompanied by proper expertise."*

The article positions:
- **AI as precision-agriculture enabler** for tailored inputs by plot and plant.
- **AI as rural-resilience enabler** for resource allocation and rural depopulation mitigation.
- **AI literacy** as a missing element — *"agricultural universities must train future leaders with multidisciplinary skills, blending agronomy, data science, ecology, law, and ethics."*
- **Regulatory caution** — *"A good regulation, policy or law is crucial for trust, safety, and fairness. However, overregulation, rules that are impossible to fulfill or laws that are soon overridden by technological evolution, might stifle innovation, prevent market introduction or make novel approaches unaffordable."*

### 6.3 What CEMA tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **CEMA represents the European equipment-maker cluster** which is structurally distinct from US equipment makers (John Deere, AGCO US operations, CNH). European equipment makers tend to cooperate more openly on data standards (ADAPT, ISOBUS) per `scans/2026-07-regional-industry-na-eu.md`.
- **CEMA's AI Project Team** is the institutional voice of European equipment makers on AI policy. The May 2025 statement is a substantive corpus anchor for understanding European equipment-maker AI positioning.
- **CEMA on regulation**: explicitly cautions against overregulation, naming the EU AI Act regulatory environment. This is a substantive EU-specific industry voice on the AI-vs-regulation tension.
- **CEMA's membership** (national associations for ~16 EU member states) gives the AI Project Team a pan-European institutional reach.

### 6.4 The CEMA gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on CEMA. Substantive unit candidate. CEMA is mentioned in `scans/2026-07-initial.md` and `scans/2026-07-regional-industry-na-eu.md` but never unitized.
- **CEMA's data-standard work** (ADAPT, ISOBUS, ISO 11783 for precision agriculture data exchange) is a substantive EU-specific interoperability layer not surfaced in the corpus.
- **CEMA's equipment-maker cluster units** (CLAAS, AGCO/PTx, Pöttinger, Ploeger, SDF, Kuhn, Kverneland, Lely, DeLaval) are at varied depths in the corpus. CLAAS and AGCO/PTx are named; Lely and DeLaval are named but not unitized; Pöttinger/Ploeger/SDF/Kuhn/Kverneland are mentioned but not unitized.

## 7. EU AI Continent Action Plan — the AI regulatory frame

### 7.1 What it is

Per Clifford Chance briefing (15 April 2025): *"EU AI Continent Action Plan April 2025: Key Features and Getting Involved."* The EU AI Continent Action Plan is the strategic policy document published by the European Commission on 9 April 2025. It is the EU's strategic counterpart to (a) the US White House AI Action Plan and (b) China's National Smart Farming Plan and other state-led AI strategies.

### 7.2 Key features

Per Clifford Chance briefing and EU AI Policy Monitor:
- **Five pillars**: AI Computing Infrastructure (AI Factories), Data, Skills, Regulation, Investment.
- **AI Factories**: Establish a network of **13 AI factories** across the EU to support AI startups (per data-week.eu briefing). AI Factories are public-private partnerships providing supercomputing capacity to AI startups.
- **Data Union Strategy**: companion strategy positioning data as strategic infrastructure.
- **AI in Science Strategy**: companion strategy positioning AI as research infrastructure.
- **Apply AI Strategy** (per EU AI Policy Monitor): the adoption-component of the AI Continent Action Plan, focused on driving AI adoption across sectors including agriculture.
- **Two AI Gigafactories** announced (€10 billion investment) for training frontier AI models.

### 7.3 What the EU AI Continent Action Plan tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **AI Factories** are the EU's structural commitment to providing supercomputing capacity to AI startups — including agritech AI startups. The 13-AI-Factory network is the EU's hyperscaler-substrate counterpart (analogous to the US hyperscaler substrate documented in `scans/2026-07-hyperscaler-substrate.md`).
- **Apply AI Strategy** is the EU's adoption-side counterpart to AI Factories. Agriculture is one of the named sectors for Apply AI adoption.
- **Two AI Gigafactories** (€10B) are at frontier-AI training scale; whether these will host agritech AI training is structurally significant but not yet documented.
- **EU sovereign AI ecosystem framing** (per EU AI Policy Monitor): *"The strategy is the adoption component of the EU AI Continent Action Plan we wrote about in April. Alongside comes an AI in Science Strategy."* The framing positions the EU as a third pole alongside US (commercial hyperscaler) and China (state-led hyperscaler) AI architectures.

### 7.4 The EU AI Continent Action Plan gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on the EU AI Continent Action Plan. Substantive unit candidate.
- **Agriculture-specific Apply AI Strategy** content is not surfaced in the corpus. The Apply AI Strategy is the EU's adoption-side document for AI across sectors; whether it includes named agritech AI adoption targets is the next-cycle work.
- **EU AI Factories × agritech AI startup access** is structurally significant. EIT Food FAN startups theoretically have access to AI Factory compute. Whether this is operationalised is next-cycle work.

## 8. The EU regulatory layer (announced, deferred)

These are the EU AI / data / climate regulatory instruments that shape European agritech AI activity but are deferred to a separate scan because each is structurally large:

- **EU AI Act**: entered into force August 2024; phased implementation through 2026-2027. World's first comprehensive AI regulation. Affects every European agritech AI vendor and farmer-cooperative data platform. **No unit, no gap**.
- **EU Code of Practice on AI**: companion to EU AI Act for general-purpose AI providers (signed by major AI providers 2025). **No unit, no gap**.
- **European AI Office** (established 2024): implementation body for EU AI Act. **No unit, no gap**.
- **EU Carbon Removal Certification Framework** (entered into force 2024): first EU-level voluntary carbon certification with MRV requirements. Affects agricultural carbon-AI deployment. **No unit, no gap**.

These four instruments together form the **EU regulatory substrate** for agritech AI activity. The user-requested scope for this scan is the **funder / institutional layer**; the regulatory layer is the natural follow-on scan (option A from the menu I offered).

## 9. EAFRD — the European Agricultural Fund for Rural Development

### 9.1 What it is

The European Agricultural Fund for Rural Development (EAFRD) is the **funding instrument of the second pillar of the EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)**. EAFRD 2021-2027 budget: approximately **€95 billion** total EU rural-development funding across the seven-year period.

Per fi-compass.eu: *"The European Agricultural Fund for Rural Development (EAFRD) is the funding instrument of the second pillar of the EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)."*

### 9.2 What EAFRD tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **EAFRD is the structural CAP funding source for digital agriculture** in member states. Each member state's **CAP Strategic Plan 2023-2027** uses EAFRD to fund digital-agriculture adoption, AI deployment, and precision agriculture at the farm level.
- **Member-state implementation varies** — France's CAP Strategic Plan has a specific AI-and-digital section; Spain's CAP Strategic Plan has cooperative-federation focus; Netherlands' CAP Strategic Plan has data-cooperative (JoinData-style) emphasis.
- **EAFRD funding flows through member states**, not directly to startups. This is structurally distinct from EIT Food (direct funding to startups) and from Horizon Europe Cluster 6 (research funding to consortia).

### 9.3 The EAFRD gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on EAFRD. Mentioned in passing in CAP Strategic Plan references.
- **Per-member-state CAP Strategic Plan AI deployment scope** is a substantive gap. France, Spain, Netherlands, Germany, Italy each have AI-relevant CAP Strategic Plan components; enumerating these would fill a structural gap.

### 10.1 The funding-flow story

The EU institutional / funder substrate operates at multiple scales:

1. **EU institutional early-stage pipeline** (EIT Food): small grants + corporate follow-on pathways for startups.
2. **EU institutional research-and-deployment funding** (Horizon Europe Cluster 6 + Mission Soil): multi-million-euro grants for consortia and Living Labs.
3. **EU institutional strategic frame** (EU AI Continent Action Plan + EU AI Act): policy frame for AI adoption and deployment.
4. **Member-state CAP funding** (EAFRD via CAP Strategic Plans): implementation funding at farm and cooperative level.
5. **EU farmer and cooperative federation** (Copa-Cogeca): institutional voice + project partner.
6. **EU equipment-maker industry association** (CEMA): institutional voice on equipment-maker AI.

Each layer has named programmes, named deadlines, and named decision-makers. The activity follows the funding flow: where EU institutions fund, AI-related activity happens.

### 10.2 The substrate comparison with US and China

| Substrate | EU institutional / funder | US | China |

| Startup pipeline | EIT Food FAN (65 startups/year, 6 countries) | AgFunder + university tech transfer; venture-funded | National Smart Farming Plan + WAICO multilateralisation |

The EU substrate is the **most multi-layered** of the three. The US substrate is **commercial-vendor-led with state-research-supported**. The China substrate is **state-led with commercial-vendor-follow-on**. The EU substrate is **multi-stakeholder with EU-institutional-coordination**.

### 10.3 What the substrate layer tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Where EU institutions fund, AI-related activity happens**: EIT Food FAN cohorts, Horizon Europe Cluster 6 consortia, Mission Soil Living Labs, Copa-Cogeca project partnerships, CEMA AI Project Team positions.
- **Where member states implement CAP, AI deployment follows**: France CAP Strategic Plan, Spain CAP Strategic Plan, Netherlands CAP Strategic Plan each have AI-relevant components.
- **Where the EU AI Continent Action Plan calls for AI Factories and Apply AI Strategy, agritech AI startups have new substrate access**: 13 AI Factories provide supercomputing; Apply AI Strategy is the adoption counterpart.
- **Where Copa-Cogeca and CEMA weigh in on EU AI Act and AI Continent Action Plan consultations, farmer and equipment-maker positions shape the regulatory frame**: Copa-Cogeca's EU-FARMBOOK and CEMA's AI Project Team statements are the institutional voice anchors.

## 12. New contested claims surfaced

- **C-134 (new):** EIT Food is the primary EU institutional funder for European agritech AI startups. Counter: EIT Food is one of several funders (Horizon Europe Cluster 6, EAFRD, member-state funds); the "primary" claim is overstated.
- **C-135 (new):** Horizon Europe Cluster 6 funds European agritech AI at scale. Counter: Cluster 6 funds a wide range of food-and-environment research; agritech-AI-specific funding is a subset of Cluster 6; the "scale" claim is overstated without per-topic enumeration.
- **C-136 (new):** EU Mission Soil Living Labs will achieve the 100-Living-Lab-by-2030 target. Counter: at current pace and given the multi-actor requirement, the 2030 target is at risk; partial achievement is the more likely outcome.
- **C-137 (new):** Copa-Cogeca represents all European farmers on AI governance. Counter: Copa-Cogeca represents 22M farmers through national member associations; specific farmer-AI positions vary by member state and by farm type.
- **C-138 (new):** CEMA's AI Project Team is the EU equipment-maker voice on AI. Counter: CEMA represents national equipment-maker associations (16 EU member states); specific equipment-maker AI positions vary by national association and by individual member company.
- **C-139 (new):** EU AI Continent Action Plan's AI Factories will be the EU's hyperscaler-substrate counterpart. Counter: AI Factories are public-private partnerships providing supercomputing capacity, not hyperscaler commercial-cloud services; the structural counterpart framing is partial.
- **C-140 (new):** EAFRD is the largest single EU funder of digital agriculture deployment. Counter: EAFRD is large but flows through member-state CAP Strategic Plans with varied implementation; direct EAFRD funding for AI deployment is small.
- **C-141 (new):** The EU institutional / funder substrate is the most multi-layered of the three (US, China, EU). Counter: US substrate has multiple layers (USDA + Cooperative Extension + land-grant universities + state agriculture departments + AEM); China substrate has multiple layers (state-led + commercial-vendor + academic + multilateral); the "most multi-layered" claim is comparable but the qualitative character differs.
- **C-142 (new):** European agritech AI activity follows EU funding flow. Counter: European agritech AI activity has significant corporate-led (Bayer, Cargill, Danone, Mars, Mondelēz) and venture-capital-led components independent of EU institutional funding; the "follows funding flow" framing overstates the EU institutional determinative role.

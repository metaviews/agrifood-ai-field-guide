## 2. CGIAR — the corpus's primary multilateral institutional anchor

### 2.1 What it is

CGIAR is the **international agricultural research body**, with headquarters near Montpellier, France, and major research centers including the International Livestock Research Institute (ILRI) in Nairobi. CGIAR coordinates 15 international agricultural research centers, including:

- **ILRI** (International Livestock Research Institute, Nairobi)
- **IRRI** (International Rice Research Institute, Philippines)
- **CIMMYT** (International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center, Mexico)
- **ICARDA** (International Center for Agricultural Research in the Dry Areas)
- **ICRISAT** (International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics)
- **CIP** (International Potato Center)
- **IITA** (International Institute of Tropical Agriculture, Nigeria)
- **AfricaRice** (Africa Rice Center)
- **World Vegetable Center**

CGIAR has been the corpus's primary institutional anchor for African agricultural research since 1971.

### 2.2 CGIAR's smallholder-centred framing

CGIAR's framing is structurally distinct from vendor-led deployment:

> *"tailored for people who rarely feature in the AI revolution: those who grow, raise, and fish for the food that feeds the world"* (per CGIAR AgriLLM launch announcement, 9 July 2025)

The framing centres smallholder farmers, extension agents, and local-language deployment. Distinct from US (corporate-vendor-led), EU (regulatory-led), and China (state-led) deployment patterns.

### 2.3 The April 2025 Nairobi event

Per CGIAR announcement (16 May 2025):

- **CGIAR Science Week side event** titled *"AI-Powered Innovation: Accelerating Research for Agri-Food System Transformation"*
- **April 2025** — held at the United Nations compound in Nairobi
- **250+ participants in person and virtually**
- **4-hour deep dive** into AI in transforming agriculture — seed to satellite, chatbot to crop field

### 2.4 Named speakers and substantive content

- **Shalini Gakhar** (IRRI data scientist): *"This future is already here, but the challenge is not technology — it's trust, readiness, and responsible deployment."*
- **Aisha Walcott-Bryant** (Head of Google Research Africa): AI tools already saving lives — real-time flood forecasts deployed in 90 countries, wildfire alerts, hyper-local weather predictions via Google Search
- **Abigail Anka** (Research Software Engineer, Google AI): building-mapping tool evolved into agricultural field-boundary detection
- **Andrea Monsalve** + **Satish Nagaraji** (CIMMYT): generative AI tools localized for farmers with low literacy + no internet + low trust in conventional platforms

### 2.5 The named deployment examples

Per CGIAR April 2025 event:

- **In Bihar (India), Kenya, and Mexico**: farmers ask questions on WhatsApp in local dialects + receive personalized audio advice from AI-trained systems
- **AI-powered phone calls** through interactive voice response (IVR) systems for data collection
- **Feedback loops** — farmer responses feed back into AI models, making them smarter with every interaction

The deployment pattern is structurally distinct from smartphone-default precision agriculture (US/EU) and from state-led smart farming (China) — **voice-first + low-bandwidth + local-language + feedback-loop-driven**. This is the **smallholder-side design pattern** that the cluster unit framed.

## 3. AgriLLM — CGIAR's flagship AI deployment

### 3.1 What AgriLLM is

AgriLLM is the **AI agricultural advisory platform** developed by CGIAR in partnership with the United Arab Emirates' **AI71**. Per CGIAR announcement (9 July 2025):

> *"AgriLLM is an ambitious project aimed at equipping the agricultural community, including researchers, policymakers and smallholder farmers, with tailored AI tools and models. Unlike general-purpose AI tools, AgriLLM is rooted in scientific rigor, grounded in CGIAR knowledge and local realities, and tailored for people who rarely feature in the AI revolution: those who grow, raise, and fish for the food that feeds the world."*

### 3.2 The Q&A pair workshops at ILRI Nairobi

- **First workshop: 9 June 2025** — 25+ in-person participants from ILRI; produced **360+ Q&A pairs**
- **Second workshop: 12 June 2025** — 70+ virtual and in-person participants from CGIAR centers; produced **500+ Q&A pairs**

### 3.3 Named CGIAR centers participating

- ILRI (Nairobi)
- ICARDA (International Center for Agricultural Research in the Dry Areas)
- ICRISAT (International Crops Research Institute for the Semi-Arid Tropics)
- CIP (International Potato Center)
- IITA (International Institute for Tropical Agriculture, Nigeria)
- IRRI (International Rice Research Institute, Philippines)
- AfricaRice
- CIMMYT (Mexico)
- World Vegetable Center

### 3.4 The Q&A generation process

Three-phase structured process:

1. Generate broad agricultural topics relevant to various user personas
2. Create realistic, needs-based questions from perspectives of farmers, extension agents, policymakers
3. Teams collaborate on evidence-based answers with citations

Goal: **500 Q&A pairs per CGIAR center** to launch AgriLLM's AI-powered assistant with region-aware, role-specific responses.

### 3.5 COP30 chatbot prototype target

**Working chatbot prototype expected at COP30** (November 2025 / 2026 cycle) — positioning CGIAR and its partners at the forefront of AI-powered agricultural transformation.

The COP30 prototype is **the corpus's most-substantive multilateral AI deployment milestone** for the period.

### 3.6 The UAE partnership

- **UAE AI71**: AI platform developed by Abu Dhabi's AI71
- **US$200 million UAE-Gates-CGIAR partnership** (per LinkedIn press)
- **CGIAR AI Hub hosted in Abu Dhabi with AI71 as core technology partner**
- Frames AgriLLM as part of "Abu Dhabi's AI Ecosystem for Global Agricultural Development"

The UAE × CGIAR partnership is structurally distinctive: **a Gulf-state AI vendor partner with a multilateral international agricultural research body** — the partnership pattern is **multilateral + bilateral with Gulf state**, distinct from Chinese-bilateral (BRI/DSR) and from EU-bilateral (EU AI Act + DG CONNECT).

### 3.7 AgriLLM next steps

- Collect additional Q&A pairs from CGIAR + initiate Q&A pairs collection with **FAO**
- Finalize post-processing + curation; expand training/test sets; proceed to second round of fine-tuning
- Deploy the fine-tuned model on the AI Assistant
- Develop context-aware retrieval capabilities (location-specific, crop-specific)
- Develop onboarding flow to extract information about the user

### 3.8 What AgriLLM tells us about CGIAR's institutional frame

- **Multilateral deployment model** — not vendor-led, not state-led
- **Knowledge-grounded** — rooted in CGIAR scientific publications + local realities
- **Smallholder-centred** — designed for people who rarely feature in the AI revolution
- **Voice-first + low-bandwidth + local-language design** — feedback loops from farmer responses
- **Public-good framing** — open-source, accessible globally
- **Partnership-based** — UAE AI71 (technology), Gates Foundation (funding), FAO (data), CGIAR centers (knowledge)

The AgriLLM pattern is **the corpus's most-substantive multilateral / institutional anchor for African agritech AI**. Worth naming in any talk about African agritech AI deployment, and structurally distinct from vendor-led (US/EU), state-led (China), and commercial-cluster-led (Sub-Saharan Africa commercial cluster) deployment patterns.

## 4. AGRA — the corpus's primary deployment-of-record anchor

### 4.1 What AGRA is

Per AGRA + Rockefeller Foundation:

- **AGRA** = Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa
- **Founded 2006 by The Rockefeller Foundation + Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation**
- Started in "breadbasket region" — Ghana, Mali, Mozambique, Tanzania
- **Became independent organization in 2012** with uniquely African board + governance
- By 2010, expanded to **13 African countries**

### 4.2 AGRA's substantive deployment scale

Per Rockefeller Foundation retrospective:

- **15 million smallholder farmers** trained
- **50,000+ square miles** of farmland restored
- **562 new seed varieties** commercialized
- **800+ projects** supported
- **42 public policy reforms** advocated
- **$1.3 billion** in new investments mobilized

The deployment scale is **the corpus's most-substantive African smallholder-deployment record**. AGRA is structurally the deployment-of-record anchor for Sub-Saharan African smallholder agricultural transformation.

### 4.3 The current Mastercard Foundation partnership

Per Mastercard Foundation 2025:

- **Countries**: Ghana (primary) + Tanzania + Kenya
- **Focus areas**: Digital + youth inclusiveness, capacity, participation in Ghana's agrifood system transformation
- **Programmes**:
  - Expand access to financial services for smallholder farmers in Tanzania, Kenya, Ghana by using agribusinesses as a method of financing
  - Support agricultural market information systems and payment innovations to serve farmers with financial + non-financial services
  - Share financial inclusion models through research, documentation, dissemination

### 4.4 AGRA's named programmes

- **Soil Health Initiative** — soil fertility restoration
- **AGRA-PASS** (Programme for Africa's Seed Systems) — 562 new seed varieties commercialized
- **Centre for African Leaders in Agriculture (CALA)** — leadership development
- **Youth in Agribusiness** — Mastercard Foundation partnership focus
- **Policy + state capability work** — 42 public policy reforms

### 4.5 The cropin-india.md reference + corpus context

Per `units/cropin-india.md`:

> *"AGRA (Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa): deployment in Mozambique, Mali, Burkina Faso, Nigeria, Ghana, Tanzania; reached 2,197 farmers with climate-smart agriculture knowledge (15-year chronicle)."*

The 2,197 farmer figure represents one specific climate-smart agriculture programme; the broader AGRA deployment covers 15 million smallholder farmers across 13 countries.

### 4.6 What AGRA tells us about African agritech AI deployment

- **Deployment-of-record scale** — AGRA's 15 million smallholder farmers, 50,000+ square miles, 562 seed varieties, $1.3 billion mobilized is the corpus's most-substantive African deployment anchor
- **Multilateral + bilateral + private foundation funding model** — Rockefeller + Gates + Mastercard are the primary funders
- **African-led governance** — became independent in 2012 with uniquely African board
- **Public policy advocacy** — 42 public policy reforms demonstrate institutional influence
- **Programme diversity** — Soil Health + Seed Systems + CALA + Youth in Agribusiness + Policy work

AGRA is **the corpus's most-substantive multilateral-deployment anchor** for Sub-Saharan African agritech AI. Distinct from CGIAR's research-led multilateral frame (AgriLLM), AGRA is **deployment-led** with the substantive deployment-of-record scale.

## 6. African Union Continental AI Strategy — the corpus's primary African multilateral AI policy frame

### 6.1 What it is

Per AU Continental AI Strategy document (July 2024):

- **Full title**: *Continental Artificial Intelligence Strategy: Harnessing AI for Africa's Development and Prosperity*
- **Endorsed by AU Executive Council** at 45th Ordinary Session, **18-19 July 2024, Accra, Ghana**
- **H.E. Dr. Amani Abou-Zeid** (AU Commissioner for Infrastructure and Energy) — leadership
- **Ms. Souhila Amazouz** (Senior Digital Policy Officer) — coordination
- **UNESCO** — substantive technical support
- **5 focus areas**:
  1. Harnessing AI's benefits for African people, institutions, private sector, countries
  2. Addressing risks associated with increasing use of AI (governance, inclusion, diversity, human rights, gender equality, dignity, safety)
  3. Building AI capabilities (infrastructure, data, skills, research, innovation, vibrant startup ecosystem)
  4. Stimulating investment (regional coordination, international partnerships)
  5. Fostering cooperation
- **15 policy recommendations**

### 6.2 The substantive framing

The strategy frames AI as:

> *"an Africa-centric, development-oriented and inclusive approach around five focus areas notably: harnessing AI's benefits, building AI capabilities, minimising risks, stimulating investment and fostering cooperation."*

The substantive commitment:

> *"Africa is laying the necessary foundations to become an integral participant in the AI revolution by optimising the benefits of AI and unlocking its vast potential to empower citizens."*

### 6.3 The 15 named national AI strategies

Per AU Continental AI Strategy document:

- **Rwanda** (2023) — *"Africa's AI lab and responsible AI champion"*
- **Senegal** (2023) — national development plan-aligned

- **Mauritius** (2018)
- **Benin** — AI and Bigdata Strategy
- **Ethiopia, Ghana, Kenya, Mauritania, Morocco, Nigeria, South Africa, Tanzania, Tunisia, Uganda** — significant steps to define AI policies + establish institutions

### 6.4 Morocco's substantive AI infrastructure

- **UNESCO Category II centre** (November 2023)
- Royal Institute for Strategic Studies of Morocco published *"Trusted Artificial Intelligence: a lever for change in favour of accelerated development in the Kingdom"*

### 6.5 The 5 named African regional AI ecosystems

Per AU strategy + cross-references:

- **Smart Africa Alliance** — Olivier Gakwaya named contributor
- **ECOWAS** (Economic Community of West African States) — Marie Nde Sene named contributor
- **ECCAS** (Economic Community of Central African States) — Guichard Tsangou-Wanvoukissa named contributor

- **AUDA-NEPAD** (AU Development Agency-New Partnership for Africa's Development)

### 6.6 What the AU Continental AI Strategy tells us about African agritech AI

- **The first pan-African AI policy frame** — endorsed at highest AU level (Executive Council)
- **Agricultural AI is named as a priority area** — *"AI applications in agriculture, education, healthcare, public service delivery, climate change, and peace and security"*

The AU Continental AI Strategy is **the corpus's most-substantive African multilateral AI policy frame**. Worth naming in any talk about African AI policy or multilateral AI coordination.

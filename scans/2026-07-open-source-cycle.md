## 1. The Mozilla State of Open Source AI Report (July 14, 2026)

**Headline findings:**

- **950+ developers surveyed globally** — substantive sample.
- **Performance gap with proprietary models has narrowed to 3%** — open-source AI is no longer playing catch-up.
- **Costs have fallen 50x in three years** — open-source infrastructure has become dramatically cheaper.
- **Open models power ~33% of real-world AI usage but capture only 4% of revenue.** The value is real; the revenue isn't flowing back to the open ecosystem.
- **China and East Asia lead in open-source AI adoption at 89%**, far ahead of the West. Open source is treated as a cornerstone of national strategy.
- **12 new national AI strategies launched last year; 47 countries restrict foreign processing for critical workloads.** The geopolitical picture is shifting fast.
- **EU, Canada, India back open ecosystems with public investment** — treating AI infrastructure as a strategic asset.
- **79% of developers use open models, only 51% have deployed them in production** (vs 63% for closed models). The gap is *infrastructure*, not quality.
- **"The real fight has moved beyond the model"** — the layer that matters most is the agentic harness (the software between people and models).
- **93% of users approve AI agent requests by default** ("consent fatigue").

**Mozilla's framing (Raffi Krikorian, CTO):** "Open source AI has reached a turning point. It's no longer about expanding access to models; it's about who has the power to shape, audit, and improve them. Without investment in the infrastructure, tooling, and governance around open models, we risk locking in a system where only restrictive, closed AI can scale."

**Why this matters for the field guide:** The Mozilla report provides the substantive data anchor for any talk about open-source AI in agrifood. The 3% performance gap + 4% revenue capture combination is the load-bearing structural finding — *open source is real and competitive on capability, but the economic sustainability model is unsolved*.

## 2. Open Source Ethos in Agricultural Heritage

**A Growing Culture, "The Open Source Ethos" (January 25, 2023):**

> "Manifestations of open are seen in our agricultural heritage, owned by all and fabricated over generations. The boundless mixing of genetic materials seen in open pollination boosts the overall vigour of plants and regenerates the abundance of nature. There is also a long history of open in many traditional cultures around the world. Local knowledge, often collectively inherited, shared orally, and passed over jurisdictional borders and geographies, is the backbone of traditional agriculture and biodiversity preservation as farmers save and trade seeds."

**The conceptual bridge:** Open source software movement → open access scholarly movement → open-pollination biology → Mycorrhizal networks (Wood Wide Web) → traditional seed saving. The "open" ethos has multiple genealogies; the agricultural / biological lineage is older than the software lineage.

**Why this matters for the field guide:** Talks that frame open-source AI in agrifood as a *cultural fit* (rather than as a foreign technical import) draw on this lineage. The A Growing Culture framing lets the presenter move from "open source software is a technical choice" to "open source software is a contemporary expression of an agricultural heritage that has been open for millennia."

## 3. How Open Source Already Weaves Through the Field Guide

### 3.1 Open-source standards layer

- **OADA** (`units/oada-open-ag-data-alliance.md`) — open-source interoperability standards project. RAML API specification on GitHub. Inspired by IETF / W3C / Apache standards tradition. "Distributed, rather than centralized, network model."
- **JoinData** (`units/joindata-netherlands.md`) — non-profit data cooperative; not open-source software in the Mozilla sense but operates on open cooperative / commons principles.
- **GAIA (Generative Artificial Intelligence for Agriculture)** (`units/la-ferme-digitale-gaia.md`) — Pillaud's open-source core of agricultural data. The most explicitly open-source AI project in the field guide.

### 3.2 Open data framework layer

- **CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance** — open framework, multilingual translations (Spanish, Vietnamese, Māori, German, Khmer). Substantive academic uptake (1,808 citations on Carroll et al. 2020).
- **FAIR Principles** (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Re-usable) — open data principles from the scientific community.
- **GODAN 2.0** (`units/open-data-ecosystem.md`) — Global Open Data for Agriculture and Nutrition. "Advances a holistic framework for transforming food systems through open data, youth-led innovation, and inclusive digital infrastructure."
- **CGIAR Platform for Big Data in Agriculture** — open data, FAIR principles, Responsible Data Guidelines.
- **Open Data Ecosystem** unit captures this layer.

### 3.3 Open cooperative / commons models

- **JoinData** (Netherlands) — first agricultural data cooperative.
- **AGUAPAN** (`units/indigenous-data-sovereignty.md`) — Association of Guardians of the Native Potato of Central Peru. Custodian-farmer network with cooperative cultural fit.
- **NAPDC / Ag Data Coop** (`units/napdc-national-ag-producer-data-cooperative.md`) — US federally-funded cooperative. National Agricultural Producers Data Cooperative.
- **La Ferme Digitale / GAIA** (`units/la-ferme-digitale-gaia.md`) — French agtech association; co-operative / commons heritage.
- **EMILI Innovation Farms** (`units/emili-innovation-farms.md`) — industry-led non-profit; farmer-cooperative adjacency.
- **AIVA Network** (`units/aiva-network.md`) — multi-actor coalition (FCC + EMILI + WHIN); farmer-centric validation infrastructure.

### 3.4 Open Indigenous Data Governance

- **CARE Principles** — already noted.
- **IEEE 2890-2025** — Recommended Practice for Provenance of Indigenous Peoples' Data. World's first global standard for Indigenous data provenance. Operationalises CARE into industry specifications.
- **Indigenous Navigator** (IWGIA) — open methodology for Indigenous communities to monitor rights implementation. EU-funded.
- **ONAMIAP** (Peru) — Indigenous women's organisation implementing Indigenous Navigator.
- **WIPO Treaty on Intellectual Property, Genetic Resources and Associated Traditional Knowledge** (2024) — open multilateral framework.

### 3.5 Open-source implementation examples in agriculture

- **FarmOS** — open-source farm record-keeping application. Web-based, modules for livestock, field records, inventory.
- **FarmVibes.AI** — Microsoft Research's open-source multi-modal geospatial ML for agriculture.
- **eLocust3** (FAO) — open-source platform for real-time locust tracking. Used by FAO and national locust-control agencies.
- **GAIA** (Pillaud / La Ferme Digitale) — open-source core of agricultural data, generative AI agents for agriculture.
- **Ag Data Coop / NAPDC** — open framework, public-private-academic partnership.
- **AIVA Network** — multi-actor validation infrastructure.

### 3.6 Open-source challenges named in the field guide

- **Mozilla 2026**: Only 51% of open models deployed in production. *Infrastructure is the gap, not quality.*
- **OpenAgri (EU, April 2024)**: "A large proportion of OS/free projects have not continued to have an active community and tend to fizzle out over time. Projects developed with limited funding often struggle to maintain momentum."
- **Mozilla 2026**: Open-source captures 4% of revenue — *economic sustainability is unsolved*.
- **Field guide's Pillaud framing**: "Unicorns do not exist in agriculture. The French agri-tech industry is not doing well, and funding from private investors remains insufficient. Using open source and developing digital commons are the obvious solution."

## 4. The Bridge Between Open Source Software and Agricultural Cultural Ethos

**The conceptual move:** Software open source has cultural preconditions. Free / open-source software requires (a) developers willing to contribute to public goods, (b) governance models that recognise contribution, (c) licensing infrastructure (GPL, MIT, Apache, Creative Commons), (d) capital for maintenance (often the gap).

**Agricultural cooperative / commons has parallel preconditions.** Cooperatives require (a) farmers willing to participate in collective action, (b) governance models that distribute benefit fairly, (c) legal infrastructure (cooperative law, mutual insurance law), (d) capital for sustainability (often the gap).

**The substantive bridge:** The Mozilla report's "the gap is in infrastructure" finding maps directly to the agricultural cooperative experience: cooperative infrastructure is often the binding constraint, not the underlying productive activity. Pillaud's "unicorns do not exist in agriculture" maps to Mozilla's "open source captures only 4% of revenue" — both name the same structural problem (sustainable funding models for non-extractive production).

**Open Source Programme Offices (OSPOs) for Good:** The UN / FAO conference framing is the institutional expression of this bridge. Open source + Sustainable Development Goals + Digital Public Goods Alliance + FAO certification. The Vincent Martin (FAO) framing: open source is essential for SDG achievement and equitable AI deployment.

## 5. The Mozilla 2026 Findings Applied to Agrifood

Five Mozilla findings have direct application to the field guide's content:

### 5.1 Performance gap (3%) is solved. The remaining problem is infrastructure.

Field guide application: *Open-source AI in agrifood is technically feasible.* The remaining work is deployment infrastructure, integration with existing farm management systems, farmer-friendly interfaces, and economic sustainability of open-source maintainers. A talk about open-source AI in agrifood should not engage with "is it good enough?" — that question is largely settled — but with "what infrastructure makes it usable?"

### 5.2 4% revenue capture

Field guide application: *Open-source AI vendors in agrifood are unlikely to achieve venture-scale returns.* This validates Pillaud's "unicorns do not exist in agriculture" framing. The Mozilla data + Pillaud's framing together establish that the *funding model* for open-source AI in agrifood is structurally different from proprietary AI in other sectors.

### 5.3 China and East Asia at 89%

Field guide application: *The Chinese agritech AI ecosystem is more open-source-intensive than the Western ecosystem.* The China cycle already surfaces this — XAG, Pinduoduo, and Alibaba all have substantial open-source / open-data components in their agritech work. Worth naming in any talk about global agritech AI.

### 5.4 Agentic harness is the new battleground

Field guide application: *Open-source AI infrastructure (not models) is where governance work happens.* The Mozilla report's framing that "the layer that matters most is the agentic harness" maps to the field guide's data-governance / data-rights-framework schema — the work isn't really about which LLM, it's about who controls the harness. The dark-data unit, the proprietary-farm-data unit, and the Indigenous Data Sovereignty unit are all about this layer.

### 5.5 93% default-approval of AI agents

Field guide application: *Consent fatigue is real.* The field guide's practitioner reality (Nelson quote, Andrew Nelson: "I've read the terms and conditions. But giving some of that information to the person that I'm having to buy a three-quarter of a million-dollar machine from just doesn't sit quite right.") captures this. The Mozilla report's 93% default-approval statistic is the structural claim; the producer quote is the practitioner-grounded version of the same observation.

## 6. The Substantive Synthesis

The field guide has, through its existing units and scans, *built up* the open-source / cooperative / commons thread without naming it as such. The Mozilla 2026 report provides the substantive data anchor for this thread. The A Growing Culture "Open Source Ethos" provides the conceptual bridge from software to agricultural heritage. The FAO / OSPOs for Good provides the institutional / UN framing.

**Three substantive observations:**

1. **Open-source AI is technically competitive in agrifood (3% gap); the deployment gap is infrastructure (51% deployed).** Mozilla 2026. The field guide's JoinData, OADA, GAIA units are all about closing that infrastructure gap.

2. **The funding model for open-source AI in agrifood is structurally different from proprietary AI in other sectors.** Mozilla 2026's 4% revenue capture + Pillaud's "unicorns do not exist in agriculture" together establish this. The cooperative / commons response (JoinData, OADA, NAPDC, GAIA, Indigenous Navigator) is not a workaround but the substantive alternative.

3. **The agentic harness is the governance layer.** Mozilla 2026's "the real fight is now the harness" maps to the field guide's data-governance / data-rights-framework work. The dark data, proprietary farm data, and Indigenous Data Sovereignty units are all about the harness, not the model.

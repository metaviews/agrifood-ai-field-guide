# Regional Scan: Latin America and the Caribbean вЂ” July 2026

**Status.** Fourth regional scan. Extends `scans/2026-07-initial.md` and `scans/2026-07-regional.md`.

**Scope.** Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC) вЂ” Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, Peru, Colombia, Chile, Costa Rica, and the broader regional institutional layer (IICA, IDB, CAF, FAO LAC, EMBRAPA). Caribbean specifics are under-surfaced; flagged as evidence-collection gap.

**Why this scan now.** The initial scan's geographic balance claim flagged Latin America as under-represented; the regional scans covered Canada, China, and Global South / Indigenous sovereignty. Latin America has both a robust critical voice (IT for Change / FIAN) and a substantial mainstream institutional layer (IICA-led Digital Agriculture Week 2025), which makes it a meaningful test of whether the existing taxonomy holds across more diverse institutional landscapes.

**Method.** Substantive reading of foundational references in each current; named critical voices read in full; matrixes built from scan evidence, not from speculation.

## 1. The currents in LAC specifically

### 1.1 Mainstream institutional / industry-aligned

The institutional layer in LAC has its own coherence вЂ” distinct from OECD-EU and from China, because the regional bodies (IICA, IDB, CAF, FAO LAC) coordinate explicitly across the LAC region.

- **IICA + IDB + CAF + Bayer + AWS вЂ” AI Day at Digital Agriculture Week 2025** (San JosГ©, October 2025). 200+ participants. Coverage includes precision farming, automation, data use, connectivity, productivity, sustainability, and organisational culture. The framing is uniformly *AI as a tool for inclusive digital transformation*, with explicit attention to connectivity as a barrier.
- **EMBRAPA** (Brazil) вЂ” 25-year history of precision agriculture; recent work on quantum computing for agriculture; partnership with Senai Cimatec for AI / robotics / IoT / photonics.
- **PROCISUR** (Cooperative Program for Technological Development of Agrifood and Agro-Industry in the Southern Cone) вЂ” regional technical cooperation, named alongside IICA / IDB at DAW 2025.
- **World Agri-Tech South America Summit** (2024) вЂ” convening role.

The mainstream framing emphasises *AI for inclusion and resilience*, but in operational terms it converges with the OECD-EU framing: AI as a tool, with named barriers (connectivity, data quality, training) and named solutions (regulatory frameworks, public R&D investment).

### 1.2 AgriTech startup / venture

Distinctly visible in LAC. Agrosmart (Brazil), Kilimo (Argentina), TerraFirma (Puerto Rico), PineSORT and AinnovaTech (Costa Rica), the BiobГ­o smart irrigation pilots (Chile). Several of these are at *meaningful scale* вЂ” Agrosmart reports 100,000+ farmers across Latin America, Kilimo 2,000+ across 7 countries.

The deployment pattern is *venture-funded platform serving SME and smallholder farmers*, with smartphone / SMS interfaces and a focus on yield / water / cost optimisation. This is structurally distinct from the OECD-EU startup pattern (where agritech is more often capital-intensive and industrial-scale) and from the Chinese vendor pattern (where state-affiliated platforms dominate).

### 1.3 Critical / rights-based

**The IT for Change / FIAN essay** by Monsalve SuГЎrez and Seufert вЂ” *The Big Tech Takeover of Food Systems in Latin America* (2022) вЂ” is the most important Latin American critical voice we've found. Load-bearing points:

- AgriTech was introduced to LAC by the agribusiness sector, *not* by smallholder farmers. Argentina and Brazil have 75% of AgriTech companies in the region; Microsoft-Monsanto joint investment fund (2016); BASF's AgroStart; Agtech Garage in Piracicaba.
- The pandemic accelerated digital tool adoption among smallholders, but *the underlying infrastructure is under-built*: 67% LAC population internet access, but only 13% broadband; rural-urban gap 25% on average, widening to 43% in Peru, 36% Colombia, 30% Mexico.
- Microsoft's TV White Spaces Project and Colombian connectivity pledge (4 million peasants) flagged as *both* an enabler and a corporate-strategic move.
- The essay frames explicitly against UNDROP (UN Declaration on the Rights of Peasants) and UNDRIP (UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples).

This is the Latin American analog to IPES-Food's *Head in the Cloud* вЂ” same framing logic, region-specific evidence, distinct actors and named institutional entry points.

### 1.4 Indigenous / peasant

Two distinct streams:

- **AGUAPAN Peru** вЂ” the custodian-farmer network preserving 1,000+ local potato varieties, named in the IPES-Food report as a farmer-led innovation example. Operates through community seed conservation and benefit-sharing agreements.
- **Indigenous Navigator Peru case** (December 2025, ONAMIAP-IWGIA) вЂ” Quechua and AshГЎninka communities using community-generated data to influence municipal governance on water scarcity, territorial integrity, food sovereignty. Documents the *data в†’ decision-making* path for Indigenous-led advocacy.

The Indigenous Navigator case is the most concrete example we've found of the *Indigenous data sovereignty* framework being operationalised at the municipal governance level in agrifood contexts. It anchors the IDSov critical-voice tag with a substantive Latin American example distinct from the US/Canadian IDSov work.

## 3. LAC-specific contested claims and gaps

Extending the existing list:

- **C-013 (new):** "AgriTech is a tool for smallholder inclusion in LAC." (Mainstream institutional framing, Borgen Project style.) Counter: IT for Change вЂ” AgriTech was introduced by agribusiness; smallholder benefit is contested; underlying connectivity infrastructure is under-built.
- **C-014 (new):** "Microsoft-style connectivity projects extend AI benefits to smallholders." Counter: IT for Change вЂ” these are also corporate-strategic moves; benefit flows need independent verification.
- **C-015 (new):** "LAC agritech startups reach smallholder farmers at scale." Agrosmart reports 100,000+ farmers. Counter: IT for Change вЂ” "reaching" and "meaningful benefit" are different claims; smallholder access to platforms в‰  smallholder governance of platforms.
- **G-013 (new):** Caribbean-specific agritech activity. Jamaica, Trinidad, Dominican Republic under-surfaced. Strong evidence-collection problem.
- **G-014 (new):** LAC-specific animal production AI beyond dairy. Poultry, beef, aquaculture AI work under-represented.
- **G-015 (new):** Agrosmart and Kilimo вЂ” independent verification of yield / water-savings claims. Vendor-reported figures (60% water reduction Agrosmart, 20% Kilimo) circulate widely; independent verification at scale not surfaced.
- **G-016 (new):** UNDROP / UNDRIP-aligned AI deployment in LAC. Frameworks exist; operationalised deployments from this frame are sparse.

## 5. What this scan asks of the taxonomy

The existing taxonomy v2 holds up reasonably well across the LAC evidence. Three observations worth noting:

1. **`capital-intensity` tag earned its place here.** Agrosmart reports 100,000+ farmers вЂ” the tag `smallholder` is meaningful even at that scale because the design assumptions (smartphone-default, basic-feature-phone fallback) are different from industrial deployments. The capital-intensity tag distinguishes correctly.
2. **`critical-voice: food-sovereignty` and `indigenous-data-sovereignty` both earned places** in distinct LAC units. The Monsalve/Seufert essay carries `food-sovereignty`; the Indigenous Navigator case carries `indigenous-data-sovereignty`. The sub-tag distinction is doing real work.
3. **`policy-instrument` tag surfaces in LAC.** IICA's DAW 2025 is a strategy instrument; UNDROP and UNDRIP are international framework instruments. The tag should be applied to entries that *are* policy instruments, not just to deployments.

One **taxonomy revision worth considering for the next iteration:**

- **Add an explicit `actor-type` cross-cutting tag** alongside `actor`. The current `actor` field is a free-text name; adding `actor-type` with values like `vendor`, `state-agency`, `multilateral-body`, `farmer-led`, `Indigenous-organisation`, `critical-civil-society`, `academic`, `farmer-cooperative`, `industry-association` would let the KB filter by actor type. The LAC scan surfaces this as a need because we now have 30+ actors spanning 8+ types and the pattern matters.

This is the first taxonomy revision the regional scan has *required* (rather than suggested) since the v2 revision.

## 1. Why this scan now

The EU institutional / funder substrate (covered in the previous scan) tells you **where activity is funded and what counts as in-scope activity**. The EU regulatory substrate tells you **what counts as compliant activity** — the regulatory architecture that constrains and frames every agritech AI deployment. These are two sides of the same coin: the funder layer is the supply side; the regulatory layer is the constraint side.

This matters because:
- **The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation.** Phased implementation runs from August 2024 through August 2027. The 2 August 2026 milestone is **fully applicable date for most provisions** — exactly at the corpus's current date.
- **Article 99 penalties are severe** — €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher. This is the corpus's most-substantive regulatory risk figure for European agritech AI.
- **General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Code of Practice** is the practical compliance pathway for frontier AI providers — including agritech AI vendors using frontier models.
- **The Carbon Removal Certification Framework** (CRCF) is the EU's voluntary regulatory framework for carbon farming — directly relevant to agricultural carbon-AI deployment (carbon sequestration monitoring, soil MRV, regenerative agriculture verification).
- **The EU regulatory substrate is structurally distinct** from US (state-by-state + sector-specific federal regulation; FTC enforcement) and China (state-led regulation integrated with industrial policy).

The EU's regulatory architecture is **the corpus's most-developed structural example of multi-stakeholder regulation of AI** — the OECD AI Principles are international but non-binding; the EU AI Act is binding and enforceable. For European agritech AI specifically, the regulatory layer shapes what's possible, what requires conformity assessment, what gets fines, and what gets to operate without oversight.

## 2. EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) — the world's first comprehensive AI regulation

### 2.1 What it is

The EU AI Act is **Regulation (EU) 2024/1689**, adopted 2024, entered into force **1 August 2024**, fully applicable **2 August 2026** with phased implementation. Per European Commission digital-strategy site:

> *"The AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024, and will be fully applicable 2 years later on 2 August 2026, with some exceptions: prohibited AI practices and..."*

Per BSR (Business for Social Responsibility) 2025 analysis: *"The EU AI Act officially entered into force in August 2024; however, the provisions did not immediately apply. Instead, the legislation took a staggered approach, with different requirements coming into force at different times over a three-year period."*

The EU AI Act is the **world's most significant piece of legislation regulating artificial intelligence globally**. It is risk-based — different AI use cases face different regulatory burdens based on the level of risk they pose.

### 2.2 Phased implementation timeline

| Date | Provisions in force |
|---|---|
| **2 February 2025** | Prohibited AI practices; AI literacy requirements |
| **2 August 2025** | General-Purpose AI (GPAI) rules; AI Office + European Artificial Intelligence Board established; national authorities designated; GPAI Code of Practice published (July 2025) |
| **2 August 2026** | Most provisions fully applicable; transparency requirements (Article 50); regulatory sandboxes (Article 57); EU database of high-risk AI systems |
| **2 August 2027** | High-risk AI systems full requirements (Annex III: biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, justice) |

### 2.3 Article 99 — Penalties (the corpus's most-substantive regulatory risk figure)

Per BSR 2025: *"Companies using any sort of technology or software that determines or predicts people's emotions should have stopped this already, or risk fines of up to €35 million or 7 percent of global annual turnover of the preceding year, whichever is higher."*

The penalty structure distinguishes:
- **Prohibited AI practices**: up to **€35 million or 7% of global annual turnover**
- **Other violations** (high-risk AI obligations, GPAI obligations): up to **€15 million or 3% of global annual turnover**
- **Supplying incorrect information**: up to **€7.5 million or 1% of global annual turnover**

These are **the corpus's most-substantive AI regulatory penalties globally** — distinct from US sector-specific penalties (FTC consent decrees; sectoral enforcement) and China state-led enforcement (integrated with industrial policy).

### 2.4 The risk-based tier structure

The EU AI Act categorizes AI systems into four risk tiers:

- **Unacceptable risk** (prohibited): social scoring by public authorities; manipulative AI; predictive policing based solely on profiling; emotion recognition in workplace and education (with limited exceptions); biometric categorization inferring sensitive attributes.
- **High risk** (Annex III): AI used in critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration, justice. Subject to conformity assessment, technical documentation, risk management, human oversight, post-market monitoring.
- **Limited risk** (transparency requirements): chatbots, deepfakes, biometric categorization, emotion recognition (non-prohibited contexts). Subject to disclosure obligations.
- **Minimal risk** (no specific obligations): AI-enabled video games, spam filters, etc.

### 2.5 What "high-risk AI" includes for agritech AI

The Annex III list does **not explicitly include agriculture**. However, agriculture-adjacent high-risk categories include:
- **Critical infrastructure** (Annex III, point 1) — AI for water, gas, heating, electricity supply. Could include agricultural water infrastructure.
- **Environmental monitoring** (per Article 6(2) and Annex III) — AI for monitoring emissions, biodiversity, and natural resources. Could include soil monitoring AI (relevant to EU Mission Soil Living Labs).
- **Food safety** (per Article 6(2)) — AI for ensuring food safety. Relevant to food processing AI, traceability AI.
- **Machinery Regulation interplay** — per OECD 2026: *"Provide guidance on the interplay and application of the EU AI Act and Machinery Regulation: clarify how AI regulations apply to agricultural machinery"* (Machinery Regulation 2023/1230 entered into force January 2027).

Per OECD 2026 (Progress in Implementing the EU Coordinated Plan on AI, Volume 2, Chapter "AI in agriculture"): *"This chapter assesses AI uptake in the agricultural sector in the European Union – with a focus on AI-powered agricultural robots, predictive analytics and crop, and soil and livestock monitoring."* The OECD's substantive mapping of EU AI uptake in agriculture.

### 2.6 Article 57 — AI regulatory sandboxes

Per artificialintelligenceact.eu: *"According to Article 57 of the AI Act, each Member State must establish at least one AI regulatory sandbox at the national level by 2 August 2026. This post..."*

AI regulatory sandboxes are **controlled environments where innovators can test new AI products or services under regulator supervision**. Each EU member state must establish at least one by 2 August 2026. Sandboxes are particularly relevant for agritech AI startups (precision agriculture, autonomous robots, predictive analytics) that need to demonstrate compliance in a real-world setting without immediately facing full regulatory requirements.

### 2.7 EU AI Act peer-reviewed academic anchor

Per Tandfonline / European Journal of Risk Regulation, October 2025: *"The EU AI Act and the Food System: How the European Union AI Act Applies to Agrifood"* (peer-reviewed academic article). The substantive academic anchor for EU AI Act + agrifood specifically.

The peer-reviewed framing: the EU AI Act applies to agrifood systems where:
- AI is used for **safety components** of machinery (Machinery Regulation interplay)
- AI is used for **food safety** monitoring and traceability
- AI is used for **environmental monitoring** including soil
- AI is used for **emotion recognition** in livestock welfare contexts (limited exceptions possible)

### 2.8 What the EU AI Act tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Most agritech AI is not high-risk under the EU AI Act.** The risk-based tier structure means most agricultural AI applications (precision agriculture, predictive analytics, autonomous robots outside the high-risk categories) face minimal regulatory burden.
- **AI used in agricultural machinery** faces the AI Act + Machinery Regulation interplay (OECD 2026 explicit call). This is the corpus's most-substantive regulatory complication for agricultural AI.
- **AI used for food safety monitoring** faces high-risk obligations (Annex III, Article 6(2)). This affects TOMRA (`units/tomra-na-food-sorting.md` analogue for EU food processing), Key Technology, Ellab, and other food-sorting AI vendors.
- **AI used for environmental monitoring** (soil, biodiversity, water) faces high-risk obligations under Article 6(2). This directly affects EU Mission Soil Living Labs and EIT Food FAN startups working on soil monitoring AI.
- **Penalties of €35M / 7% global turnover** are structurally significant — they are the corpus's most-substantive AI regulatory penalties globally. European agritech AI vendors and farmer-cooperative platforms must build compliance infrastructure.
- **AI regulatory sandboxes (Article 57)** are the practical compliance pathway for early-stage agritech AI startups.

### 2.9 The EU AI Act gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on the EU AI Act. Substantive unit candidate.
- **Specific agriculture-relevant AI Act guidance** is still being drafted. The OECD 2026 chapter explicitly notes the need for guidance on the Machinery Regulation interplay. Worth tracking as future guidance emerges.
- **National AI regulatory sandbox implementation** (Article 57) by member state is substantive next-cycle work — which member states have established sandboxes, and which include agritech AI specifically.
- **Fines actually imposed under the EU AI Act** are zero as of July 2026 (per AI Act timeline; prohibited practices were banned February 2025; first enforcement actions are expected 2026-2027).

## 3. General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (GPAI Code of Practice)

### 3.1 What it is

The General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Code of Practice is the **EU AI Act's voluntary compliance pathway for general-purpose AI model providers**. Published July 2025, applied to GPAI from 2 August 2025. Developed by the AI Office + a wide range of stakeholders.

Per European Commission press corner (10 July 2025): *"General-Purpose AI Code of Practice now available... general-purpose AI, which will enter into application on 2 August 2025... Henna Virkkunen, Executive Vice-President for Tech..."*

Per artificialintelligenceact.eu (last updated 14 August 2025): *"The AI Act Code of Practice (introduced in Article 56) is a set of guidelines for compliance with the AI Act. It is a crucial tool for ensuring compliance with the EU AI Act obligations, especially in the interim period between when General Purpose AI (GPAI) model provider obligations came into effect (August 2025) and the adoption of standards (August 2027 or later)."*

### 3.2 Three chapters structure

The Code has three chapters:

- **Transparency** (applies to all GPAI model providers): documentation, downstream provider information, training data summary.
- **Copyright** (applies to all GPAI model providers): policy to respect EU copyright law (particularly regarding training data).
- **Safety and Security** (applies only to providers of GPAI models with systemic risk): model evaluation, systemic risk assessment and mitigation, cybersecurity protection, serious incident reporting.

### 3.3 Signature mechanism

GPAI model providers can sign the Code of Practice to demonstrate compliance with EU AI Act obligations until European standards come into effect (expected August 2027 or later). Signing is **voluntary but practically significant** for downstream deployment.

Per Wikipedia (cited for signature tracker): *"The EU AI Act, adopted in 2024, established a risk-based regulatory regime... 'Commission publishes full list of AI companies signed up to Code of Practice.'"*

Named signatories as of 2025-2026 include frontier AI providers (Anthropic, Google, OpenAI, Microsoft, Meta for their EU-serving models) and downstream providers including European agritech AI vendors using frontier models.

### 3.4 What the GPAI Code of Practice tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Most European agritech AI vendors are not GPAI model providers** — they are downstream deployers of GPAI models. The Code applies to them indirectly through transparency and copyright obligations of their model providers.
- **Agritech AI vendors building foundation models or large domain-specific models** (e.g., agricultural vision-language models) may be subject to GPAI obligations.
- **The Transparency and Copyright chapters** shape what training data summaries must be published — directly relevant for agricultural AI vendors using farm data for training.
- **The Safety and Security chapter** (only for systemic-risk models) affects frontier AI providers serving European agritech AI use cases.

### 3.5 The GPAI Code of Practice gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on the GPAI Code of Practice. Substantive unit candidate.
- **List of GPAI model provider signatories** as it evolves is next-cycle work.
- **Agritech AI vendors' Code of Practice compliance** as a practical matter is thin in publicly available documentation.

## 4. European AI Office + European Artificial Intelligence Board + Scientific Panel + Advisory Forum

### 4.1 The governance architecture

The EU AI Act establishes a multi-body governance architecture:

- **European AI Office** (established 2024, operational August 2025): enforcement body for GPAI obligations. Located within DG CONNECT (European Commission Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology).
- **European Artificial Intelligence Board** (established 2024): member-state coordination body for AI Act implementation. Each EU member state designates a national authority.
- **Scientific Panel of Independent Experts** (established per EU AI Act): independent expert body supporting enforcement on GPAI models and systems. Per artificialintelligenceact.eu (7 July 2026): *"The scientific panel of independent experts (the Scientific Panel) is a body established under the EU AI Act to support the enforcement of rules on general-purpose AI (GPAI) models and systems. It sits within the Act's broader governance architecture as one of the..."*
- **Advisory Forum** (established per EU AI Act): general advisory body to the European Commission and the AI Board. Per artificialintelligenceact.eu (19 June 2026): *"The Advisory Forum (the Forum) is a general advisory body to the European Commission and the AI Board, established to provide technical expertise, advise them, and to contribute to their tasks under the EU AI Act."*

### 4.2 What the governance architecture tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **The European AI Office** is the structural counterpart to the US AI Safety Institute (within the Department of Commerce). Direct comparison: US AI Safety Institute is advisory + technical; EU AI Office has enforcement authority.
- **Member-state national authorities** vary by country. France's CNIL (Commission Nationale de l'Informatique et des Libertés) is a substantive data-protection authority that intersects with EU AI Act enforcement. Spain's AESIA (Agencia Española de Supervisión de la Inteligencia Artificial, established 2024) is the Spanish national AI authority.
- **The Scientific Panel of Independent Experts** provides substantive technical expertise — potentially relevant for agricultural AI safety questions.
- **The Advisory Forum** provides stakeholder input — Copa-Cogeca (per `scans/2026-07-eu-institutional-funder-substrate.md`), CEMA (per same scan), and EIT Food likely have representation or input.

### 4.3 The governance architecture gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on the EU AI Act governance architecture. Substantive unit candidate.
- **Member-state national authority AI deployment** (AESIA Spain, CNIL France, etc.) is substantive next-cycle work.
- **Copa-Cogeca and CEMA submissions to the AI Office and Advisory Forum** are thin in publicly available documentation.

## 5. EU Carbon Removal Certification Framework (CRCF) — Regulation (EU) 2024/3012

### 5.1 What it is

The CRCF is **Regulation (EU) 2024/3012**, adopted 27 November 2024. Voluntary EU framework for certifying permanent carbon removals, carbon farming, and carbon storage in products. The first EU-level voluntary carbon certification framework.

Per Jones Day (March 2025): *"On November 27, 2024, the European Union ('EU') adopted Regulation (EU) 2024/3012 establishing a Union certification framework for permanent carbon removals, carbon farming, and carbon storage in products (the 'Carbon Removal Certification Framework Regulation'). This voluntary framework aims to enhance the credibility and transparency of carbon removal activities within the EU to support its climate neutrality by 2050 objectives, in line with the EU's commitments under the Paris Agreement."*

### 5.2 Three primary activities

The CRCF targets three primary activities:

- **Permanent carbon removals** that sequester CO₂ for several centuries (geological storage, durable products).
- **Carbon farming** through terrestrial or coastal management that increases carbon sequestration in soil (including soil emission reduction activities).
- **Carbon storage in products** where CO₂ is trapped for **at least 35 years** (e.g., timber, agricultural fibers in buildings).

### 5.3 Quality criteria (the corpus's most-substantive MRV regulatory anchor)

Per Jones Day (March 2025): *"Quality criteria require, in particular, an accurate measurement of the amount of CO₂ removed and calculation of the period during which the CO₂ will be stored to ensure that the above-mentioned long-term requirements are met. The carbon removal, farming or storage activities must also respect biodiversity and zero-pollution objectives, and in general 'do no significant harm to the environment' as well as contribute to at least one sustainability objective defined by the Carbon Removal Certification Framework Regulation."*

The four key quality criteria:
1. **Quantification** — accurate measurement of CO₂ removed
2. **Additionality** — activities go beyond statutory requirements
3. **Do no significant harm (DNSH)** — biodiversity and zero-pollution compliance
4. **Sustainability** — contribute to at least one sustainability objective

### 5.4 The certification process

- **Voluntary** — certification is not required for operation, but provides credibility and access to EU markets.
- **Independent third-party verification** — certification will have to be verified by an independent third party.
- **EU-only** — only activities taking place in the EU may qualify.
- **Member state and private operator certification schemes** — Member States as well as private operators can file certification scheme approval.
- **Interoperable public registries** — certification schemes track carbon removal units through interoperable public registries.
- **Common Union-wide registry by end of 2028** — by end of 2028, interoperable public registries should be replaced by a common Union-wide registry to streamline the process.

### 5.5 The CRCF Buyers' Club

Per European Commission climate site (last updated 22 June 2026): *"Discover the CRCF Buyers' Club: an initiative that brings together buyers and suppliers of certified carbon removal and carbon farming units under the EU CRCF framework."*

The Buyers' Club is an EU Commission initiative to facilitate the buyer-seller matching for certified carbon removal units. Substantively significant for agricultural carbon-AI deployment because it provides the demand-side market infrastructure.

### 5.6 What the CRCF tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Carbon farming is the agricultural AI wedge.** Soil carbon sequestration monitoring, soil emission reduction verification, and regenerative agriculture verification all require AI-mediated measurement and reporting. The CRCF provides the regulatory frame for these AI applications.
- **EU Mission Soil Living Labs** (per `scans/2026-07-eu-institutional-funder-substrate.md`) deploy AI for soil monitoring — the CRCF provides the certification framework for the carbon-removal outcomes these Living Labs produce.
- **CRCF MRV requirements** (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification) are the regulatory demand for soil-monitoring AI, satellite imagery analysis AI, and farm-level carbon-accounting AI.
- **The EU-only restriction** is structurally significant — non-EU agritech AI vendors cannot directly benefit from CRCF certification, but their products can be used by EU operators.
- **Voluntary framework + Buyers' Club** is the EU's structural commitment to **market-creation rather than market-mandate** for carbon removals. Distinct from US (state-level voluntary carbon markets) and China (state-led carbon market — per `units/china-deepening-scan-rural-revitalization.md` for the China-side frame).

### 5.7 The CRCF gap

- The corpus does not have a standalone unit on the CRCF. Substantive unit candidate.
- **Certification methodologies** are being drafted by the Commission in delegated acts (per Jones Day: *"compliance will have to be verified by an independent third party... and in upcoming delegated acts which will define certification methodologies"*). Tracking the specific methodologies is next-cycle work.
- **First certified CRCF projects** are likely emerging in 2026-2027. Tracking the named projects and AI-vendor involvement is substantive future work.
- **CRCF Buyers' Club named buyers and suppliers** is a substantive gap — likely members include major food processors, retailers, and EU member-state programmes.

### 6.1 The regulatory-flow story

The EU regulatory substrate operates at multiple layers:

1. **AI regulation layer** (EU AI Act + GPAI Code of Practice + governance bodies) — the corpus's most-substantive AI regulatory architecture globally.
2. **Climate regulation layer** (CRCF) — voluntary EU framework for carbon-removal and carbon-farming certification; agricultural carbon-AI is the agricultural wedge.
3. **Machinery regulation layer** (Machinery Regulation 2023/1230, entered into force January 2027) — affects agricultural equipment-maker AI deployments; OECD 2026 explicitly notes the need for guidance on the AI Act + Machinery Regulation interplay.
4. **Data protection layer** (GDPR) — affects all EU agritech AI vendors processing farmer data.
5. **National implementation layer** — member-state national authorities (AESIA Spain, CNIL France) implement and enforce EU regulations within national jurisdictions.

### 6.2 The substrate comparison with US and China

| Substrate | EU regulatory | US | China |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI regulation | EU AI Act (comprehensive, binding, phased 2024-2027) + GPAI Code of Practice + AI Office | Sector-specific (FTC consent decrees; sectoral enforcement); White House AI Action Plan; state-level (Colorado AI Act, California SB 1047 vetoed) | State-led regulation integrated with industrial policy (CAC measures, MIIT requirements) |
| Penalties | €35M or 7% global turnover (most severe); €15M or 3%; €7.5M or 1% | Variable by sector (FTC consent decrees typically in millions; sectoral enforcement variable) | Variable; can include operational restrictions, fines, and criminal liability |
| AI Office equivalent | European AI Office (enforcement) + AI Board (member-state coordination) + Scientific Panel + Advisory Forum | US AI Safety Institute (advisory + technical, Department of Commerce); NIST AI Risk Management Framework | Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC); MIIT; MOST |
| Climate regulation | CRCF (voluntary carbon farming certification) + EU ETS (existing) | State-level voluntary carbon markets; California cap-and-trade | National carbon market (since 2021); expanding |
| Machinery regulation | Machinery Regulation 2023/1230 (entered into force January 2027) | OSHA equipment safety; sectoral | State-led equipment safety |
| Data protection | GDPR (binding, comprehensive, 2018) | Sector-specific (HIPAA, COPPA, state-level CCPA, etc.) | Cybersecurity Law + Data Security Law + Personal Information Protection Law (state-stewarded) |

The EU regulatory substrate is **the most comprehensive and binding globally** — distinct from US sector-specific enforcement and China state-led enforcement integrated with industrial policy.

### 6.3 What the regulatory substrate tells us about European agritech AI activity

- **Most agritech AI is not high-risk under the EU AI Act**, but specific agricultural AI applications (machinery, food safety, environmental monitoring) face high-risk obligations.
- **Penalties of €35M / 7% global turnover** are the corpus's most-substantive AI regulatory penalties globally — European agritech AI vendors and farmer-cooperative platforms must build compliance infrastructure.
- **AI regulatory sandboxes (Article 57)** are the practical compliance pathway for early-stage agritech AI startups — each EU member state must establish at least one by 2 August 2026.
- **GPAI Code of Practice** is the voluntary compliance pathway for GPAI model providers — affects frontier AI providers serving European agritech AI use cases.
- **CRCF MRV requirements** drive soil-monitoring AI, satellite imagery analysis AI, and farm-level carbon-accounting AI. The regulatory demand for agricultural carbon-AI is structural.

The regulatory substrate is the **constraint side** of the EU institutional / funder substrate (the supply side). Together they form the EU's complete agritech AI architecture.

## 9. New contested claims surfaced

- **C-143 (new):** The EU AI Act is the corpus's most-substantive AI regulatory architecture globally. Counter: structurally significant but implementation is just beginning (most provisions apply 2 August 2026); actual enforcement record is zero as of July 2026. The architecture is binding but the practice is unproven.
- **C-144 (new):** Most European agritech AI vendors are not subject to high-risk EU AI Act obligations. Counter: agricultural AI deployed in food safety, environmental monitoring, or as safety component of machinery faces high-risk obligations; the "most" framing is correct but the substantive obligations are still significant.
- **C-145 (new):** The GPAI Code of Practice is a voluntary compliance pathway. Counter: while technically voluntary, GPAI model providers face practical pressure to sign because downstream deployers and EU customers expect compliance demonstration.
- **C-146 (new):** EU AI regulatory sandboxes are the practical compliance pathway for early-stage agritech AI startups. Counter: sandboxes are at member-state implementation level; not all member states have established agritech-relevant sandboxes.
- **C-147 (new):** The CRCF is the corpus's most-substantive agricultural carbon-AI regulatory anchor. Counter: the CRCF is voluntary and in early implementation; the actual carbon-AI MRV regulatory demand depends on the delegated acts being drafted.
- **C-148 (new):** The EU regulatory substrate is more comprehensive than US or China substrates. Counter: US has sector-specific regulation that is binding within sectors (FDA for food safety, USDA for agricultural chemicals, EPA for environmental); China has state-led integrated regulation; the "most comprehensive" claim depends on whether "comprehensive" means cross-sectoral or sectoral depth.
- **C-149 (new):** EU AI Act penalties of €35M / 7% global turnover are the most severe globally. Counter: structurally the most severe on paper; actual enforcement is zero; China's CAC and MIIT can impose operational restrictions and criminal liability that may be more substantive in practice.
- **C-150 (new):** The EU regulatory substrate constrains European agritech AI activity. Counter: regulation also enables activity by establishing clear rules, creating markets (CRCF Buyers' Club), and supporting compliance demonstration for downstream partners.
- **C-151 (new):** Copa-Cogeca and CEMA have substantive institutional voice in EU AI Act and CRCF consultations. Counter: position papers exist but the substantive impact on final regulatory text is not documented; institutional voice is real but influence is hard to verify.
- **C-152 (new):** The EU AI Act + Machinery Regulation interplay creates complexity for agricultural AI deployment. Counter: the EU Commission has committed to providing guidance (per OECD 2026), and the practical complexity may be lower than feared.

## Why this scan matters for talks

## Critical context

- **The EU AI Act implementation is in early stages** as of July 2026. Most provisions apply from 2 August 2026; high-risk AI systems full requirements apply 2 August 2027. Actual enforcement record is zero as of July 2026.
- **The GPAI Code of Practice is voluntary but practically significant.** Signing is the practical compliance pathway for GPAI providers before August 2027 standards.
- **The CRCF is voluntary and in early implementation.** Certification methodologies are being drafted in delegated acts; first certified projects likely emerging 2026-2027.
- **Verification posture is H1** for all four regulatory instruments — named articles, named bodies, named regulations, named timelines. But implementation in practice is at the very beginning.
- **The structural comparison with US and China substrates** shows the EU is **the most comprehensive regulatory substrate** but at the cost of implementation complexity. Worth naming in any talk about global AI governance architecture.

## Content

Taco Bell × Omilia voice AI is *the largest deployed voice-AI drive-thru footprint in the United States*. As of July 2026:

- **890+ US Taco Bell drive-thru locations** across **38 states** are running Omilia voice AI for automated order taking.
- The partnership with Omilia dates to 2023. Taco Bell × Yum also tried a parallel deployment with NVIDIA voice-AI frameworks in March 2025 (~500 restaurants) — Yum slowed that deployment in August 2025 after public trolling incidents (orders for ~18,000 cups of water etc.) and customer complaints, and consolidated on the Omilia stack.
- Yum! Brands launched *Byte by Yum!* in 2024 — a proprietary SaaS platform integrating AI, POS, and online ordering across Taco Bell, KFC, and Pizza Hut; the voice-AI surface runs on this stack.
- The unit represents the largest *deployed* AI ordering surface in US QSR (as of mid-2026).

## What this unit is doing in the taxonomy

The load-bearing *deployed-at-scale* example for generative AI in US restaurant ordering. Counter-anchor to the *failed/discontinued* McDonald's × IBM unit. Together they form the public case study of voice-AI drive-thru: one chain scaled successfully, another pulled the technology.

## Why it matters for talks

- Concrete deployment numbers (890+ units, 38 states) are unusually strong — verified across Restaurant Dive (Jul 7 2026) and Businesswire (Jul 7 2026).
- The Yum × NVIDIA → Omilia pivot is a teachable moment on *production reliability* vs *technology stack novelty*: the more novel NVIDIA deployment failed in market, the more conservative Omilia stack scaled.
- Public trolling incidents (18,000 cups of water) demonstrate the *consumer-vs-AI* surface that vendor presentations systematically underplay.

## Critical context the unit does not sanitise

- The AI ordering surface is a *consumer-facing product*, but Taco Bell did not collect public consent for the underlying voice recording (drive-thru audio is captured for AI training and quality control). No publicly visible consumer data-governance instrument beyond Yum's general privacy notice.
- Public trolling incidents are *operational* failures but also *fun* consumer behaviour — the surface has no clear escalation path beyond crew intervention.
- The same vendor deployment (Omilia) is being marketed to other QSR chains — the consumer experience is becoming standardised in ways consumers cannot see.

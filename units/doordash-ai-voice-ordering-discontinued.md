## Content

DoorDash ended its *AI voice-ordering* product pilot for restaurant clients at the end of **April 2025**, after launching it in August 2023. The product was a near-2-year run.

The product aimed to automate phone-order handling for restaurant staff; DoorDash's stated reasons for discontinuation were *product-market fit* and *customer demand*. Bloomberg (May 20 2025) and Yahoo Finance (May 20 2025) confirm the shutdown.

DoorDash's separate *consumer-facing* generative AI initiatives continue: *Ask DoorDash* (AI-powered recommendation and cart-building assistant, live in 2025–2026) is a distinct product from the discontinued restaurant phone-order pilot.

## What this unit is doing in the taxonomy

Second high-profile *discontinued* voice-AI deployment in the US agrifood consumption sector (alongside McDonald's × IBM). Useful for talks making the point that consumer-facing voice AI in food has a *higher* discontinuation rate than the vendor narrative suggests.

## Why it matters for talks

- Documents a second-order voice-AI failure mode: *business-to-business* deployment (restaurants adopting the AI to handle phone orders) failed even where direct-to-consumer voice AI (Taco Bell, Wendy's) is succeeding.
- The fact that DoorDash continues to invest in *consumer-facing* generative AI (Ask DoorDash) while shutting the *restaurant-facing* voice pilot shows the failure mode was the channel (phone ordering) not the technology (LLM voice).
- DoorDash also launched an *internal AI research lab* and an *MCP (Model Context Protocol) tool* that lets AI agents transact on the platform — the discontinuation of the voice pilot did not slow DoorDash's broader AI investment.

## Critical context the unit does not sanitise

- Phone-order AI is a labour-displacement question (replacing restaurant host-staff with automation). The discontinuation is consistent with product-market-fit reasons but does not resolve the underlying labour question — DoorDash may revisit the space with different technology.
- The contrast with Taco Bell and Wendy's shows that *who owns the consumer surface* matters: drive-thru ordering is owned by the restaurant; phone ordering for delivery is intermediated by DoorDash. The latter is harder to scale because restaurants don't see direct consumer value.

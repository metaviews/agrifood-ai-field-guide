## Content

MyFitnessPal acquired *Cal AI* (a photo-based calorie-tracking app using computer-vision AI) and is integrating the technology into the MyFitnessPal app. Independent coverage indicates Cal AI's photo food-logging features are being rolled out within MyFitnessPal, likely first on iOS.

Separately, MyFitnessPal launched *Coach* in April 2026 — a conversational AI coaching feature for Premium and Premium+ users, initially iOS-only, English-only, six countries. *Coach* is a generative-AI conversational surface that handles on-demand coaching queries.

The two products together represent MyFitnessPal's move from a logging app to an AI-augmented nutrition platform. Independent academic study (Li 2024, peer-reviewed) found MyFitnessPal's food-image recognition at **97% accuracy**, the highest of seven AI-enabled food image recognition apps tested.

## What this unit is doing in the taxonomy

The largest *consumer-installed* nutrition-logging AI product in the US by user base. Anchors the *logging* cell of the consumer nutrition × AI taxonomy, parallel to ZOE's *microbiome+glucose* cell and Levels Health's *CGM-only* cell.

## Why it matters for talks

- Documents the *acquisition-driven* AI strategy in consumer nutrition (MyFitnessPal bought Cal AI rather than building) — a common pattern across the NA consumer-AI space.
- The Coach feature is *iOS-only, English-only, six countries* at launch — a small initial footprint for a Premium-only product. Documents the *premium-tier* gating that characterises consumer AI products.
- The 97% accuracy figure (academic, peer-reviewed) is a useful counter-point to the *vendor-only* claims that dominate the consumer nutrition AI space.

## Critical context the unit does not sanitise

- MyFitnessPal's *data breach history* (2018 breach exposed 150 million user accounts) is a *sovereignty flag*: the same vendor now offers AI products that process user food photos, weight logs, and conversational coaching queries.
- The Premium-tier gating means the AI features are accessible only to paying subscribers — a digital-equity concern.
- Photo-based calorie tracking has known accuracy limits even at 97%: the remaining 3% error, multiplied across daily meals, can produce systematic bias in the consumer's self-understanding of intake.

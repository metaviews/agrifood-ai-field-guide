## Content

Hungryroot is a US direct-to-consumer grocery and meal-kit delivery service that uses AI to personalise weekly orders for subscribers. The AI selects roughly **70% of what customers buy** (vendor figure). The company reached ~$250M revenue (Modern Retail reporting).

The product combines grocery items with ingredient-kits and prepared meals in a single weekly delivery. The AI personalisation engine takes customer preferences, dietary restrictions, and feedback (thumbs up/down on items) to compose each week's order without explicit shopping.

This is a *consumer-facing* AI deployment where the AI is *not* the conversational interface (unlike Walmart Sparky or Ask Instacart) — it is the *implicit ordering mechanism*. The consumer sees a curated weekly basket, not an AI chat.

## What this unit is doing in the taxonomy

Anchors the *implicit-AI / agentic grocery* cell — AI that selects on the consumer's behalf without a conversational surface. Distinct from the *conversational* cells (Sparky, Ask Instacart, PC Express in ChatGPT).

## Why it matters for talks

- Documents the *agentic commerce* pattern in grocery delivery — AI doing the cart composition, not just the chat.
- 70% AI-selection is a high implicit-AI rate; consumers may not realise that the majority of their weekly basket is AI-curated.
- Useful for any talk comparing *conversational* vs *agentic* consumer AI in grocery.

## Critical context the unit does not sanitise

- A 70% AI-selection rate means the consumer's *autonomy over what they eat* is heavily mediated by the vendor's model. The model's optimisation criteria (vendor profit margin, customer retention, perceived satisfaction) are not transparent.
- Hungryroot's pricing and product mix are vendor-controlled; the AI does not necessarily optimise for *nutrition* or *food sovereignty* but for *basket completion and retention*.
- The product is subscription-gated and US-only — digital-equity concern similar to other consumer AI nutrition products.

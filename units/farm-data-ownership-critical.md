## Content

The farm data ownership conversation has *three distinct critical lenses* that have developed since the mid-2010s. Each lens diagnoses a different problem; each proposes a different response. Worth distinguishing because conflating them loses substantive analytical traction.

### Lens 1 — IPES-Food corporate concentration lens

IPES-Food's *Head in the Cloud* (Feb 2026) names the corporate concentration of food system data as a structural concern. Monsalve Suárez and Seufert (FIAN International Secretariat) extend this specifically to agritech in Latin America in the *Big Tech Takeover of Food Systems* essay (per IT for Change, 2025).

**The diagnosis:** Big Tech's data capture of agrifood is a structural concern distinct from the technology deployment itself. The vendor model captures aggregated value that should flow to farmers and to public-interest research.

**The proposed response:** Alternative innovation systems; public-interest research infrastructure; farmer data cooperatives; IDSov-aligned alternatives.

### Lens 2 — Indigenous Data Sovereignty (CARE Principles, IEEE 2890-2025)

The **CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance** — Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics — articulate Indigenous data sovereignty in agrifood contexts. **IEEE 2890-2025** standard is the operational implementation standard.

**The diagnosis:** Indigenous communities have the right to control data about their lands, crops, livestock, and food systems. Standard data governance frameworks (FAIR Principles for technical interoperability) do not address Indigenous sovereignty; CARE Principles are complementary but distinct.

**The proposed response:** CARE Principles operationalisation; Indigenous-led data infrastructure; co-design of agritech with Indigenous communities; recognition of Indigenous data sovereignty in international frameworks.

**Concrete operational anchor:** Indigenous Navigator Peru case (ONAMIAP-IWGIA), AGUAPAN Peru (IPES-Food example), Peru-China-Bhutan seed-sharing agreement.

### Lens 3 — Civil Eats / farmer advocacy / community-food-system lens

Elizabeth Vaughan (Small-Farm Tech Hub at Community Alliance with Family Farmers, CAFF), captured in Civil Eats (March 2026):
> "Is Amazon going to be our food producer 50 years from now, or Microsoft—or are we still going to have small farmers that provide for their local communities, grow culturally relevant crops, and have resilient community food systems?"

**The diagnosis:** Big Tech capture of farm data is a structural threat to small-farm / community-food-system viability. The aggregation advantage goes to vendors; small farmers are price-takers in the data layer as much as in the input layer.

**The proposed response:** Small-farm data infrastructure; community-owned data cooperatives; right-to-repair advocacy; farmer-led certification schemes (Ag Data Transparent); community-food-system resilience as a policy frame.

### The institutional response — Ag Data Transparent

Ag Data Transparent is the operational instantiation of farmer-data ownership rights within the industry. John Deere certified (first major ag player); Bayer Climate FieldView certified since 2021.

**Critical observation:** Ag Data Transparent addresses *transparency* (contract disclosure, data use disclosure), not *control* (vendor cannot sell anonymised data, vendor cannot aggregate against farmer interests). A contract can be Ag Data Transparent certified *and* still grant broad rights to anonymised data pools.

This is the gap: the institutional response addresses transparency but not control. The critical lenses (IPES-Food, IDSov, Civil Eats) all name the *control* gap as the substantive concern.

### How the three lenses differ

| Lens | Primary concern | Proposed response | Operational anchor |
|---|---|---|---|
| IPES-Food corporate concentration | Vendor capture of aggregated value | Alternative innovation systems; public-interest research | IPES-Food *Head in the Cloud*; IT for Change LAC essay |
| Indigenous Data Sovereignty | Indigenous community control of data about their lands | CARE Principles operationalisation; Indigenous-led infrastructure | Indigenous Navigator Peru; ONAMIAP-IWGIA; IEEE 2890-2025 |
| Civil Eats / community-food-system | Small-farm viability; Big Tech capture | Data cooperatives; right-to-repair; farmer-led certification | CAFF Small-Farm Tech Hub; Civil Eats reporting; Ag Data Transparent (limited) |

The three lenses are not interchangeable. Talks that conflate them lose analytical traction.

## Why it matters for talks

- The three critical lenses are distinct and worth distinguishing in any talk that engages with farm data governance.
- The Ag Data Transparent gap (transparency without control) is worth naming honestly.
- The IDSov lens is the most distinctive — it articulates a *right* (Indigenous data sovereignty) that the FAIR Principles do not address.
- The corporate concentration lens is the most developed internationally (IPES-Food).
- The community-food-system lens is the most practitioner-anchored (Civil Eats, CAFF).

## Critical context

- The three lenses are sometimes conflated by media coverage. Worth distinguishing.
- Ag Data Transparent addresses transparency, not control.
- The IDSov lens articulates a *right* that the FAIR Principles do not address.
- The corporate concentration lens is the most developed internationally (IPES-Food).
- The community-food-system lens is the most practitioner-anchored (Civil Eats, CAFF).

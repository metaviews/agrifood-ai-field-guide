## Content

Summer Sullivan is a PhD candidate in Sociology at the University of California, Santa Cruz. Her September 10, 2025 essay **"A Tale of Two Valleys: The Salad Bowl of the World Goes Digital"**, published in the Social Science Research Council's **Just Tech** publication (DOI 10.35650/JT.3087.d.2025), is the most substantive recent critical-voice contribution on US specialty-crop AI and farmworker labour in the agrifood AI field. Per memory hygiene (substantive analytical contribution over citation volume): Sullivan is included because the work engages substantively with the labour, technology, and political-economic dimensions of specialty-crop automation in the Salinas Valley — dimensions that mainstream agrifood AI reports (USDA AI Strategy, the AI Institutes' communications, vendor marketing, investment pitches) systematically avoid.

Sullivan's contribution is based on **field visits, interviews, and focus groups** in the Salinas Valley from 2021-2025, with primary ethnographic attention to romaine lettuce harvesting and adjacent specialty crops (broccoli, berries). The work is interview-and-fieldwork-based; not peer-reviewed academic publication. Citation count is small; analytical contribution is substantive.

### The five substantive findings

**1. The "holy grail" framing is industry-side, not neutral.** Sullivan documents that the phrase *"harvesting of specialty crops remains the elusive 'holy grail' of automation"* came up in multiple interviews and at conferences, including from automation promoters themselves. The framing is structural: industry-side actors are the ones naming harvest automation as the most difficult problem.

**2. The technical limits are concrete, not media-hype.** Sullivan's field research documents specific technical failure modes of automated lettuce harvesters:
- **Water-jet-knife approach**: *"spews mud into the harvest process and ruins otherwise good product."*
- **Food-safety concerns**: *"bacteria buildup at the machine's joints, where water and material can pool but which are difficult to clean."*
- **Robotic finger costs**: $500 each, frequently needing replacement due to leaks and cracks in the soft material.
- **Mistakes vs. skilled farmworkers**: machines make *"far more mistakes than a skilled farmworker, such as picking a bad quality lettuce or passing over a ripe one."*

**3. The economics do not yet work.** *"As it stands right now, hiring farmworkers for the romaine lettuce harvest is still cheaper and more reliable than the existing automated harvesters."*

**4. A Salinas Valley agriculture automation expert (interviewed by Sullivan April 18, 2025) stated head-lettuce harvest automation is currently at "Zero percent."**

**5. The labour dimension is structural, not transitional.** *"The ongoing attempts to automate romaine lettuce harvesting demonstrate the continued and unquestionable need for careful farmworker labor within so-called digital agriculture. It follows, then, that they need to be paid, treated, and valued according to the value they produce — the Salinas Valley lettuce industry would wilt without farmworkers."*

A farmworker quoted in Sullivan's focus group (December 2025): *"When they tried a machine that cut the lettuce, the lettuce was cut upwards and they tore it apart, the one with hearts [romaine]. . . . Then I had to fix it. So, people are always going to be needed."*

A roboticist interviewed by Sullivan (February 2025): *"You need hands to cut lettuce. Think about a robot with a knife. That's not happening. . . . It's just so delicate. It's a really hard nut to crack."*

### Salinas Valley context (the geographic anchor)

- **Monterey County 2024 gross specialty crop production value**: just shy of $5 billion.
- **Leafy greens production**: Salinas Valley accounts for at least 70% of US leafy greens production.
- **Romaine lettuce**: accounts for almost $1 billion of Monterey County's $5 billion specialty crop revenue (2024).
- **Labour context**: California overtime laws (Rural Migration News 30(3), July 2024); H-2A temporary migrant worker program costs; growers responding via automation push AND geographic relocation (Peru, Chile, Mexico).
- **Silicon Valley proximity**: Salinas Valley is ~1 hour from Silicon Valley. Forbes Salinas Valley AgTech Summit began 2015. Hundreds of subsequent events and partnerships.

### The field-pack labour process (the structural finding)

Sullivan documents the **field-pack labour process** for romaine lettuce — harvested, processed, and packaged in the field, requiring 14 farmworkers across 7 rows per harvest (2 per row). One "cutter" uses a knife to cut the lettuce head and remove outer leaves; the second farmworker of the pair cleans, checks for quality, and seals the packaging directly on a moving platform. The cutting requires within-second skill: *"choosing—within seconds—ripe lettuce that is not too young, damaged, or yellowing and then harvesting it without harming the tender, watery vegetable."*

Field-pack is higher-risk for growers (no processing facility for additional inspection/cleaning) but provides unique freshness and quality. This is the labour configuration that automation is attempting to displace — and that Sullivan documents is not yet displaceable at commercial scale.

## Why it matters for talks

- **The most-substantive recent critical voice on US specialty-crop AI.** Joins Carolan as a key US-side critical voice worth referencing in any talk about digital agriculture, labour, or specialty-crop AI.
- **The "zero percent" head-lettuce harvest automation finding is a load-bearing concrete datum.** Counters vendor and investor pitch decks that conflate research-stage prototypes with commercial deployment.
- **The farmworker voice (verbatim quotes from focus groups) is analytically rare in agritech discourse.** Sullivan's fieldwork centers farmworker experience — the structural inversion of vendor framing.
- **The intellectual lineage (Fairbairn, Guthman, Walker, Mitchell, Friedland) gives the work academic rigor** that the typical agritech trade press lacks.
- **The "holy grail" framing as industry-side language** is a useful diagnostic: industry actors themselves name the harvest problem as the hardest, while investor pitches often conflate progress on weeding with progress on harvest.

## Critical context

- **Sullivan is a PhD candidate, not a tenured academic.** The work is interview-and-fieldwork-based ethnography published in a media publication (Just Tech / SSRC), not peer-reviewed academic article. Per corpus hygiene: citation volume ≠ substantive contribution. Sullivan's contribution is substantive; the citation count is small.
- **The work focuses on Salinas Valley specifically.** Other US specialty-crop labour contexts (Florida berries, Washington apples, Georgia Vidalia onions, California's Central Valley table grapes) are not surfaced. The farmworker-side critical voice beyond Salinas Valley remains a gap (G-176).
- **The interview-based methodology has standard limitations** — selection of interview subjects, depth of access, time-frame of fieldwork. Sullivan addresses these in the SSRC essay directly.
- **The labour framing is consistent with the broader US specialty-crop labour-replacement narrative** — automation as response to H-2A worker costs and California overtime regulations. The structural question is whether automation is targeted at H-2A cost reduction or whether farmworker labour remains structurally necessary. Sullivan's evidence supports the latter reading.
- **The Salinas Valley has substantial existing institutional support for farmworker advocacy** (United Farm Workers, various NGOs). Sullivan is not the only voice; the corpus hygiene rule on citation volume vs. contribution still applies.

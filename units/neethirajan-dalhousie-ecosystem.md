## Content

This unit maps the actor-network around Suresh Neethirajan's distinctive Canadian academic-policy framing of agritech AI. The ecosystem has three concentric layers.

### Layer 1 — Neethirajan's own work

- **Suresh Neethirajan** (also known as Suresh Raja Neethirajan) — Dalhousie University Research Chair in Digital Livestock Farming. Dual appointment: Faculty of Agriculture + Faculty of Computer Science.
- **Mooanalytica research team** — Neethirajan's lab, "a research group, an integral part of Dalhousie University's Faculty of Agriculture and Faculty of Computer Science." Includes priority access to several research laboratories in the Netherlands.
- **The Frontiers paper** (April 2026): *Green AI for climate-smart dairy and poultry: a tiered policy framework for computational sustainability*. Five guiding principles for Green AI governance: energy transparency, boundary harmonization, architectural proportionality, climate-first design, FAIR compute metadata. Three-tier framework differentiating expectations for smallholders, commercial operations, and export-oriented value chains. 2026–2030 roadmap for funders, regulators, technology providers, supply-chain actors.
- **Cybersecurity for digital livestock** — Public Safety Canada Cyber Security Cooperation Program funding. Tested at Dalhousie's Ruminant Animal Centre and Atlantic Poultry Research Centre.
- **Biological digital twins** — distinctive research focus. Dalhousie press (2026): "Canada has a unique opportunity to lead in biological digital twins."
- **Standardization Roadmap for Energy-Efficient AI** (SSRN 2024).
- **Book**: *Transforming Agriculture through Artificial Intelligence for Sustainable Development* (Springer).

### Layer 2 — Dalhousie colleagues (same institution)

- **Dr. Craig MacEachern** — McCain Foundation Postdoctoral Fellow at Dalhousie Faculty of Agriculture. Adjacent researcher on AI in agriculture.

### Layer 3 — Adjacent Canadian voices and producers in the ecosystem

- **Jeff Torrie** — Grey County Ontario dairy farmer who deployed Lely-style milking robots in 2016 (family farm since 1869). Direct quote in The Logic: "If we didn't invest in new technology, there wasn't going to be succession. That's what it came down to." Real operational case of farmer-driven robotics adoption.
- **Dale Steele** — former Farmer's Edge consultant; precision-ag adoption research, Western Canada. Notable finding from 2017 AAFC report: "13 per cent use Wi-Fi or cellular networks to transfer equipment data to the cloud"; "39 per cent of respondents weren't comfortable sharing their data with anyone"; "only 15 per cent said they were comfortable sharing it with the government." Concrete Canadian data-governance / connectivity figures.
- **Farmer's Edge** — Canadian precision-agtech company (Steele worked there 2011-2015). Note: company has had financial difficulties since (similar to Indigo Ag pattern). Worth flagging as a Canadian-context boom-bust analog.
- **AAFC** (Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada) — federal department. 2017-2018 briefing notes obtained by The Logic via access-to-information: "Canada has generally not been a significant creator of new primary agriculture and food processing technologies." AAFC framing.

### Layer 4 — The framework positions that hold the ecosystem together

Three substantive positions from Neethirajan that animate the network:

1. **"Canada's AI strategy stops at city limits"** (Policy Options, January 2026). Rural AI deployment is *already happening* without governance. AI policy assumes urban infrastructure (reliable broadband, stable power, central oversight) that doesn't exist on farms. Critical voice: digital-equity.

2. **Green AI: "move from AI-for-agriculture to Green-AI-for-agriculture, where computational sustainability is integral to mitigation and adaptation."** The energy / emissions footprint of AI itself must be governed alongside biological emissions. Distinct from Pillaud's funding-gap framing; names *environmental cost of computation* as a structural issue.

3. **Cybersecurity as critical infrastructure.** "Food systems are critical infrastructure too. Protecting those systems may prove just as important as protecting banks." Connects agritech to national resilience framing — not just farm-level productivity.

## Why it matters for talks

- The Dalhousie ecosystem is a *Canadian-specific* critical voice that complements the IPES-Food and Pillaud framings. Talks about Canadian agritech AI should engage with it.
- The Green AI framing is the most credible Canadian-internal environmental-cost framing. Distinct from "AI helps sustainability" — names the *computational cost* of AI itself.
- The cybersecurity-as-critical-infrastructure framing connects agritech to national resilience — a framing that's structurally different from productivity / yield / efficiency.
- Charlebois's food-safety-AI framing is adjacent but tangential — his broader work is food-distribution-and-policy commentary rather than substantive AI-in-agriculture work.
- Jeff Torrie's quote on robotic milking and farm succession is a real operational case for farmer-driven tech adoption. Worth using in any talk about who actually adopts AI on farms and why.

## Critical context

- The "Canada is losing ground" framing (The Logic 2018, AAFC briefing notes) predates the *AI for All* strategy (June 2026) but the structural diagnosis — skills gaps, data privacy concerns, broadband connectivity — is the same one FCC's July 2026 framework names. Eight years of structural diagnosis; partial movement through *AI for All* and FCC Capital $2B commitment.
- Neethirajan's cybersecurity framing is recent (March 2026) and growing — this is an emerging critical lens, not yet a dominant one in the field guide.
- MacEachern is the more substantive Dalhousie-adjacent voice surfaced; broader food-policy commentary circulating at Dalhousie is tangential to the field guide's substantive AI-and-agriculture framing.
- The Logic 2018 piece surfaces: Jeff Torrie (real operational case for farmer-driven milking robotics adoption), Dale Steele (13% Wi-Fi/cellular data transfer, 39% data-sharing discomfort, 15% gov-data comfort — 2017 figures worth re-verifying), Farmer's Edge as Canadian boom-bust analog to Indigo Ag.
- Dale Steele's 13% / 39% / 15% figures (2017) on connectivity / data sharing / government data sharing are old but the structural pattern likely holds. Worth re-verifying.

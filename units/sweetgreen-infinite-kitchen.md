## Content

Sweetgreen's *Infinite Kitchen* is an automated bowl/salad makeline that uses robotics + AI/ML to assemble customer orders. The system originated from Sweetgreen's 2021 acquisition of *Spyce* (an MIT-spinout automated kitchen company; note: Sweetgreen earlier acquired *Galley* in 2019 for meal-kit logistics, but Infinite Kitchen is the Spyce technology line).

Sweetgreen reported in 2025:
- Plans for **18 of 37 net new restaurant openings in fiscal 2025 to feature Infinite Kitchen**, plus 7–9 new units and 2–4 retrofits in earlier 2024 plans.
- Q3 2025 earnings release updates: at least 25 new Infinite Kitchens planned for 2025.
- **September 2025 announcement: Sweetgreen is selling the Infinite Kitchen business (Spyce) to Wonder for $186.4M**, while retaining the right to deploy the technology in its own restaurants "with even greater [speed]".

The Infinite Kitchen is *not* a consumer-facing conversational AI surface; it is in-store automation. It is included in the taxonomy because it is the largest *deployed* AI/robotics system in US fast-casual restaurant consumption as of mid-2026.

## What this unit is doing in the taxonomy

The *consumer-facing* tag in the schema is loose here: Infinite Kitchen is *behind the counter*. It is included because the consumer experiences the AI through the *output* (the bowl that arrives) and because the technology question (AI in restaurant food assembly) is structurally parallel to consumer AI.

## Why it matters for talks

- Documents the *robotics/automation* layer of consumer-facing AI in US food — distinct from the conversational/LLM layer.
- The Spyce → Sweetgreen → Wonder divestment trajectory is a teachable pattern on *strategic refocusing* in restaurant AI.
- 18 new units in one fiscal year is meaningful scale for restaurant automation.

## Critical context the unit does not sanitise

- Restaurant automation has a *labour displacement* dimension — Sweetgreen has not publicly committed to a no-layoffs policy tied to Infinite Kitchen deployment.
- The September 2025 sale to Wonder is itself a *strategic signal*: Sweetgreen is exiting the *technology* business to focus on the *restaurant* business, while licensing back the technology. This is a common pattern but worth surfacing.

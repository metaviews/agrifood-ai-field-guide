## Content

This unit is the corpus's **critical-voice anchor for the Brazilian beef AI cluster** — the unit that the LAC deepening scan committed to drafting but wasn't, surfacing the *gap between traceability-as-corporate-commitment and traceability-as-operational-reality* in Brazilian beef supply-chain AI.

**The cluster being critiqued:** Three Brazilian beef processors — JBS, Marfrig, Minerva — operate at scale, all three are deploying supply-chain AI for cattle traceability, and all three face critical-voice scrutiny from civil-society organisations (Chain Reaction Research, Greenpeace Unearthed, Mighty Earth, Imazon Green Radar, Reporter Brasil) and from multilateral / regulatory actors (EU Forest Risk Regulation, US UFLPA-equivalent programmes). The Brazilian beef AI cluster is therefore a **cluster-with-tension**: deployment is real, deployment-as-promise-and-as-reality are *not the same*, and the critical-voice evidence demonstrates the gap.

**Critical-voice evidence base (mid-2026):**

| Source | Date | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Greenpeace Unearthed | July 2022 | JBS, Marfrig, Minerva "promise" 100% indirect-supplier monitoring by 2025; actual deployment "lagging" — full programme realisation not achieved. The three majors had hired private companies to implement customised traceability but operationally each falls short of the corporate-public commitment. |
| Imazon Green Radar 2024 ranking | 2024-2025 | Marfrig, Minerva, Rio Maria top the **direct-supplier** control ranking; processing of indirect suppliers not similarly ranked. The direct / indirect asymmetry is structural. |
| Reuters | May 2025 | Cattle-audit conformity figures: JBS cattle purchases reached 98.2% conformity in Mato Grosso. Industry-aggregate compliance figures but specific to direct-supplier tier. |
| Sevilla et al. ScienceDirect | 2025 | Peer-reviewed analysis: "The big three (JBS, Minerva and Marfrig) have all hired private companies to implement customized cattle traceability within their production supply chains" — peer-reviewed framing of the cluster's *variability* across providers. |
| Mighty Earth Rapid Response #7 | April 2026 | **JBS bought cattle from two farms linked to nearly 1,400 hectares of deforestation in the Amazon rainforest in 2025** — direct adversarial evidence of post-2025-target supply-chain continuation with embedded deforestation. |
| Reporter Brasil + Mighty Earth 2026 | 2026 | Continued deforestation-linked supply-chain exposure through Brazilian beef; AI monitoring programmes not eliminating the gap. |
| EU EUDR (EU Deforestation Regulation) | Application phase 2025-2026 onward | EU importer compliance pressure on Brazilian beef exporters — AI traceability becomes a *trade-requirement* substrate, not just a corporate-cosmetic programme. |

**The cluster-pattern critical-voice observation:** AI traceability is deploying at significant scale in Brazilian beef — Minerva's leather 200,000-animal SBCert milestone (Oct 2025), JBS's $9M COP-28 Pará-state programme, Marfrig × Agrorobótica AGLIBS cattle-soil-carbon AI pilot — but the AI programmes are *not jointly reaching the operational-clean-supply-chain goal they were framed as enabling*. The Mighty Earth April 2026 finding (1,400 ha of Amazon-deforestation-linked cattle in JBS's supply chain in 2025) is the single most quotable critical-voice evidence of this gap.

**Why the gap persists:**

1. **Direct-supplier vs indirect-supplier asymmetry.** Direct suppliers (slaughterhouses) are well-monitored; indirect suppliers (the ranches that raise cattle, often the source of deforestation) are the hard layer to monitor. The three majors' corporate public commitments target 100% indirect-supplier monitoring; their operational programmes lag behind.
2. **Cattle-movement pattern complexity.** Brazilian cattle move through multiple ranches between birth and slaughter; tracking every individual animal's birthplace, movement history, and current location requires dense RFID/infrastructure.
3. **Compliance regime lag.** EU EUDR's compliance pressure grows year-on-year, but compliance is *not the same as operational achievement* — companies can ship-and-document-without-providing-substantively-improved-practice. AI monitoring creates the *documentation substrate*; it does not by itself prevent deforestation.
4. **Critical voice vs corporate framing asymmetry.** Corporate communications emphasise AI traceability as evidence of operational commitment; critical voices show continued deforestation-linked supply. The dispute is not about *whether* AI is being deployed; it is about *whether the deployment is achieving the goal*.

## Critical context

- **The gap is structural, not corporate-specific.** No Brazilian beef processor has fully achieved indirect-supplier monitoring at scale; this is a sectoral challenge, not a JBS-specific or Marfrig-specific one.
- **The critical-voice sources are themselves not necessarily objective.** Mighty Earth, Greenpeace Unearthed, Reporter Brasil all have explicit campaign stances against industrial beef; their evidence is *structural* but should be weighed against corporate framing in a balanced talk. The Sevilla 2025 ScienceDirect paper, as peer-reviewed independent academic work, is a useful triangulation anchor.
- **EU EUDR / CBAM compliance pressure** is one of the strongest *external* drivers of Brazilian beef AI deployment. The deployment is *not* primarily driven by domestic pressure (the Brazilian domestic regulatory regime is weaker than EU); it's driven by external export-market requirements. This structural fact is itself critical to the cluster-critique.
- **The "blockchain + AI" traceability framing** is a corporate-side narrative that doesn't guarantee operational outcome. Imazon's Green Radar ranking and Mighty Earth's Rapid Response both show that documentation-substrate deployment is *not the same as* deforestation-reduction.
- **The Brazilian beef AI cluster as a whole should be characterised as cluster-pattern observable AND cluster-with-tension**, not as cluster-pattern only the positive side. The corpus's discipline: name the deployment, name the gap.
- **The voice of *the ranches themselves* is not surfaced in primary sources.** The Brazilian beef AI traceability programmes affect ranches directly; rancher-level voice on whether the programmes are workable, equitable, or correct is largely absent from the corpus's primary-source evidence. This is a gap worth tracking (G-109).

# Canada IDSov + AI in Agrifood — four-layer ecosystem map

**This scout applies the four-layer ecosystem-mapping pattern** (collaborators, institutional anchors, adjacent voices, framework positions) per project memory — same pattern previously applied to Pillaud (Sepal.ai / Le Cycle) and Neethirajan (Dalhousie). Pattern sourced from subagent 1's framework scout + the operational deployment units + the consolidation scout.

The pattern is **applied per framework** (each of the six frameworks gets its own four-layer map), then **applied at the Canadian agrifood AI deployment level** (each deployment's collaborator/anchor/voice/position map).

## 1. CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Carroll et al. 2020)

### 1.1 Collaborators (named organisations + named individuals)

**Founding co-leads:** Stephanie Russo Carroll (University of Arizona, USA); Maui Hudson (University of Waikato, Aotearoa New Zealand).

**Drafting-team named individuals:** Jan Chapman (Australian National University); Oscar Luis Figueroa-Rodríguez (COLPOS, Mexico); Jarita Holbrook (University of Western Cape, South Africa); Ray Lovett (Australian National University); Simeon Materechera (North West University, South Africa); Mark Parsons (Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, USA); Kay Raseroka (Joint Minds Consult, Botswana); Desi Rodriguez-Lonebear (U of Arizona & U of Waikato); **Robyn Rowe (Laurentian University, Canada)**; Rodrigo Sara (CGIAR/ Biodiversity International, Italy); **Jennifer Walker (Laurentian University, Canada)**.

**Canadian institutional collaborators:** Robyn Rowe + Jennifer Walker at Laurentian University, Sudbury, Ontario. **Two of 12 named co-authors are Canadian.**

**Reviewers / commenters (Canadian-named):** **Kelsey Leonard** (University of Waterloo); Tahu Kukutai; Maggie Walter; Alaska Native Policy Center / First Alaskans Institute.

### 1.2 Institutional anchors

- **GIDA (Global Indigenous Data Alliance)** — international custodian. Members from Aotearoa (NZ), Australia, Canada, Finland, Norway, Mexico, Sweden, Tonga and Samoa (diaspora Aotearoa), and the United States.
- **US Indigenous Data Sovereignty Network (USIDSN)** — US anchor.
- **Maiam nayri Wingara** — Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Data Sovereignty Collective (Australia; formed early 2017).
- **Te Mana Raraunga** — Māori Data Sovereignty Network (NZ; formed 2015).
- **Collaboratory for Indigenous Data Governance** (U of Arizona) — research infrastructure.
- **Indigenous Data Lab (indigenousdatalab.org)** — operational hub for CARE Data Maturity Model.

**No specifically Canadian institutional anchor for CARE surfaced.** FNIGC (OCAP) and ITK (NISR) operate adjacent but do not formally adopt CARE.

### 1.3 Adjacent voices

- **ITK National Inuit Strategy on Research (NISR 2018)** — Inuit-specific data governance framework. Distinct from CARE but complementary.
- **FNIGC OCAP® (1998)** — First Nations-specific. Predates CARE; informs it.
- **W3C Data Privacy Vocabularies** (data-minimisation vocabulary) — adjacent technical work.
- **Indigenous Protocol and AI Approaches (Carroll et al. 2020, A4AI white paper)** — adjacent AI-specific.
- **Mozilla Common Voice Indigenous languages** — adjacent on Indigenous data infrastructure.

### 1.4 Framework positions on AI

- **GIDA 2024 "CARE Directs Us Home" communiqué** — explicit caution against treating CARE as a static compliance checklist.
- **Operationalising paper** (Carroll et al. 2021, *Scientific Data* 8, 108) — operational framework for AI/data.
- **Indigenous Data Lab "Safeguarding Traditions, Enhancing Agriculture"** (Jennings et al. 2026) — first substantive CARE + AI + agriculture operationalisation; US-based but methodologically load-bearing.
- **CARE has not been officially adopted by any Canadian agrifood ministry or agency as of July 2026.**

## 2. OCAP® (FNIGC, Ottawa, 1998)

### 2.1 Collaborators

- **Assembly of First Nations (AFN)** — political arm; AFN Resolution 30-2025.
- **Indigenous Services Canada (ISC)** — federal funder.
- **Regional First Nations organisations** — operational implementation partners.
- **Chiefs of Ontario** — provincial First Nations political organisation; commissioned Wald 2024 *First Nations and Artificial Intelligence* paper.
- **AFN National Steering Committee (1998)** — original drafting body. **Cathryn George** credited with original "OCA" (Association of Iroquois and Allied Indians).

### 2.2 Institutional anchors

- **FNIGC (First Nations Information Governance Centre)** — incorporated 22 April 2010 (AFN Resolution #48/2009). Akwesasne, Ontario + Ottawa, Ontario. Special mandate from AFN.
- **AFN (Assembly of First Nations)** — political arm; Resolution 30-2025.
- **ISC (Indigenous Services Canada)** — federal funder.
- **CIPO (Canadian Intellectual Property Office)** — trademark registration; OCAP® and PCAP® logos August 2014; acronyms August 2015.
- **FNIGC's 1998 history page** confirms "OCAP® is **specifically First Nations**, not Indigenous broadly."

### 2.3 Adjacent voices

- **AFN submission to INDU committee on Bill C-27 (October 2023)** — critiqued AIDA as "overly individualistic, excluding any protection from community harms."
- **Wald 2024 *First Nations and Artificial Intelligence*** (Chiefs of Ontario commissioned, Ontario Ministry of Health funded) — most substantive Canadian First Nations AI policy paper; identifies OCAP application gaps for AI training data and generative AI outputs.
- **Margaret Yun-Pu Tu (2025) Policy Options (IRPP)** — substantive AI+IDSov critique.

### 2.4 Framework positions on AI

- **No FNIGC standalone statement on AI surfaced.**
- **Wald 2024 + Tu 2025** are the substantive critical voices engaging OCAP + AI.
- **AFN October 2023 submission** explicitly critiqued AIDA for being "overly individualistic, excluding any protection from community harms."

- **GIDA recognition** — includes active participation of Indigenous members in development process, Indigenous Caucus, consultation with Indigenous Peoples, ensuring open access.

## 8. Cross-cutting observations

### 8.1 Institutional density is substantial; deployment-side framework citation is thin

The framework-side institutional density is substantial (GIDA, FNIGC, IEEE SA, Mila, NISR, NCIAF, FNDGS). **The deployment-side framework citation is thin: only SIKU explicitly cites NISR + FAIR + CARE in published literature.** All other deployments (PolArctic, Salmon Vision, SmartICE, NEDA, Inuvik Tech) integrate IK + AI but do not formally cite a framework as governing document.

This is the **framework-deployment operationalisation pattern** — frameworks articulate; deployments operationalise; alignment is implicit, not explicit.

### 8.2 The four-layer ecosystem-map pattern shows where the gaps are

- **CARE Principles** — substantive collaborators, institutional anchors, and adjacent voices; **no Canadian institutional anchor.**
- **OCAP** — substantive institutional anchor (FNIGC); **no standalone AI statement.**
- **IEEE 2890-2025** — substantive institutional anchor; **no Canadian institutional anchor on working group.**
- **Mila Indigenous AI** — substantive institutional infrastructure; **no published joint CSA standard.**
- **IQ / NISR** — substantive territorial + national Inuit frameworks; **no formal framework position on AI.**
- **FAIR + CARE** — substantive bridge articulation; **no Canadian operationalisation.**

### 8.3 The collaborative frontier

The pattern suggests **where the next operational frontier is**: deployments that explicitly cite OCAP/CARE/NISR/IEEE 2890-2025 as governing documents. SIKU is the only existing operational anchor. NCIAF is positioned to operationalise an IDSov-aligned programme but has not yet articulated an AI/data-sovereignty framework (per subagent 1 finding; G-342 implicit).

**The next-cycle gap is: which operational Indigenous-led AI agrifood deployment will formally cite OCAP / CARE / NISR / IEEE 2890 in its published documentation?**

# Canada Constraint/Critical Scout — July 2026

**Status.** Scouting-only pass over the constraint/critical side of the Canadian agrifood AI picture. **No edits to the repo beyond this scout file.** Field guide material is in the existing Canada/Quebec/regional scans.

**Purpose.** Augment the existing Canada framing (which the parent agent already characterised as: AI for All strategy, EMILI/CAPI industry position, Neethirajan rural-policy critique, connectivity barrier, Indigenous data-sovereignty framework, Quebec Law 25 context only lightly, FCC as convener, vendor-data concerns) with the **named instruments, dated status, primary URLs, and the genuine absences** that are missing from current scans.

**Method.** Pulled primary sources (parliamentary, regulator, critical academic, Indigenous-led, labour/producer) for every load-bearing claim. Verified dates. Where a finding *changes* the existing Canada framing, called out the discrepancy explicitly.

**Cutoff.** Active in the last 12 months (July 2025 – July 2026). Older instruments included only when load-bearing.

## 0. Headline findings (TL;DR)

1. **Canada's flagship AI bill is dead and the AI for All strategy is a *strategy*, not legislation.** AIDA (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act, Bill C-27) died on the order paper at prorogation on **6 January 2025**. The companion CPPA (Consumer Privacy Protection Act) died with it. No replacement AI Act has been tabled as of July 2026; a privacy bill was rumoured for early 2026 but had not been introduced by mid-2026 (IAPP, Feb 2026). Canada's AI Safety Institute (CAISI), launched November 2024, is the active federal AI body. *This means the "EU-style funder+regulatory pair" cannot be mirrored cleanly in Canada — there is no Canadian equivalent of the AI Act on the near horizon.*
2. **AI for All (June 4, 2026) names agriculture as a priority sector but is *adoption-led*, not regulation-led.** The strategy's stated targets: raise AI adoption from ~12% to 60% by 2034; 250,000 new AI jobs in five years; up to 90,000 work placements; ~$2B of new programme spending (BBC summary). **No new binding AI rules** — the "trust" pillar promises new legislation on deepfakes, surveillance pricing, online safety, and stronger privacy law, but none of those bills have been tabled as of July 2026.
3. **There is *one* substantive new federal agrifood-AI regulatory move in the cycle: Health Canada's RPAS/drone pesticide application policy, June 30, 2026.** SPN2026-02 permits drone spraying of any pesticide already registered for conventional aerial application, subject to Transport Canada pilot certification. Aligns Canada with Australia and Japan. This is the *single most concrete* binding federal agrifood-AI-adjacent regulatory instrument of the cycle.
4. **The critical academic voice is now *two* Canadian scholars, not one.** Neethirajan (Dalhousie; Policy Options January 2026 "Canada's AI strategy stops at city limits" + Frontiers Sustainable Food Systems April 2026 "Green AI for climate-smart dairy and poultry") and **Dara (U of Guelph AI4Food director)** for greenhouse AI risk. A third voice, **Charlebois (Agri-Food Analytics Lab, Dalhousie)** writes a more pragmatic-critical line ("the problem isn't AI, it's data"). All three should be tracked as separate critical voices in the field guide.
5. **Connectivity remains the binding constraint, and the policy signal is *negative*** for the AI for All cycle. The Wire Report (industry source, 27 May 2026) reports the **Universal Broadband Fund (UBF) will not be renewed**; the government will rely on satellite (Telesat LEO). ISED confirmed (5 June 2026) it will **not raise** the 50/10 Mbps minimum standard. Auditor General data (2021, still the baseline): 90.9% national coverage, but only **42.9% on First Nations reserves** and 59.5% in rural/remote. The AI for All strategy promises new compute but no new rural fibre.
6. **Indigenous data sovereignty has a Canada-specific operational framework that the existing scans underuse.** OCAP (FNIGC, 2010 → CARE Principles 2020 → IEEE 2890-2025) is the framework; the **First Nations Data Governance Strategy** was submitted to Indigenous Services Canada in 2025 (AFN Resolution 30-2025). **Indigenous-led agrifood deployment** has a named institutional vehicle: NCIAF (National Circle for Indigenous Agriculture and Food), launched June 13, 2024, with FCC support. **No Indigenous-led *AI* agrifood deployment** has been documented in primary sources as of July 2026 — the framework exists; the deployed cases are absent.
7. **Labour and producer-side evidence is *thinner* than the EU/France equivalent and largely organized around two channels:** (a) **UFCW Canada** for food processing / meat packing workers (245,000+ members in food processing; submission to Senate AGFO committee on technology and labour), and (b) **National Farmers Union (NFU)** for farmer-side data rights and broadband ("Big Data in Canadian Agriculture" SSHRC synthesis, August 2021; NFU comments on regulatory reform). **The CLC (Canadian Labour Congress) submission against AIDA in 2023–2024** is the closest analogue to a French/CFDT-style AI-specific labour critique.
8. **Farm data governance is *not* ungoverned — there is now a named Canadian instrument cluster.** EMILI's Canadian Agri-Food Data Initiative (CADI) has published three legal-landscape guidebooks (Contracts April 2024; Ownership Dec 2024; **Competition & Right-to-Repair June 2025**) in collaboration with MLT Aikins, Dentons, U of Ottawa CIPPIC, CCSC (U Saskatchewan), and others. **EMILI / CAPI's ISED submission** recommends a national data strategy modelled on the UK Farm Data Principles certification. **No Canadian equivalent of the UK Farm Data Principles or EU Code of Conduct on Agricultural Data Sharing has been adopted.**
9. **Cellular agriculture** is the one AI-adjacent food-tech area where Canada *has* issued specific regulatory guidance (CFIA / Health Canada 2023–2024: most cellular agriculture products will be regulated as novel foods under the Food and Drugs Act). Worth distinguishing from agrifood AI proper, but Canada's only sector-specific AI-adjacent regulatory posture.
10. **Architecture recommendation (see §9):** **Canada does NOT warrant a clean EU-style two-sided "funder+regulatory substrate pair"** because the regulatory substrate is, as of July 2026, almost entirely *absent at the federal level*. The Canadian scan architecture should be **asymmetric / three-pronged**: (i) a **funder + convenor substrate** (FCC + EMILI + CAPI + Protein Industries Canada + CAAIN + Pan-Canadian Smart Farm Network), (ii) a **fragmented regulatory substrate** (federal AI Safety Institute + sectoral regulators + provincial gaps + Quebec Law 25 + Indigenous sovereignty frameworks), and (iii) a **critical-voice substrate** (Neethirajan + Dara + Charlebois + CCPA + FNIGC + UFCW + NFU + NCIAF + Indigenous scholars).

## 1. Federal regulatory instruments — what's live, what's dead, what's promised

### 1.1 AI Act (AIDA / Bill C-27) — **DEAD**

- **Instrument:** Bill C-27, the *Digital Charter Implementation Act, 2022*, contained three acts: the **Consumer Privacy Protection Act (CPPA)**, the **Personal Information and Data Protection Tribunal Act**, and the **Artificial Intelligence and Data Act (AIDA)**.
- **Tabled:** 16 June 2022, House of Commons, 44th Parliament, 1st Session.
- **Died:** **6 January 2025**, prorogation of Parliament following PM Trudeau's resignation. Bill never returned. Status per Parliament of Canada LEGISinfo: marked "Dead" / historical.
- **Status as of July 2026:** No replacement federal AI legislation introduced. Schwartz Reisman Institute (U of Toronto, Feb 11 2025) and Montreal AI Ethics Institute (Blair Attard-Frost, Jan 2025) both confirm "AI governance has not ground to an abrupt halt" but flag that horizontal AI legislation is unlikely before late 2027 at earliest. IAPP (27 Feb 2026) reports: observers Scassa (U Ottawa) and Karbaliotis (nNovation Counsel) say a privacy bill was reportedly ready for reintroduction but has been held up by "data sovereignty concerns" under Carney's Major Projects Office. Status: still not tabled.
- **Agrifood implications:** None directly. AIDA would have covered "high-impact AI systems" with cross-sectoral risk-mitigation, transparency, and record-keeping duties, enforced by a new AI and Data Commissioner. *Because AIDA died, every Canadian agrifood AI system currently operates without sector-specific AI risk regulation.* Quebec Law 25 covers privacy only.
- **Sources:**

  - ISED companion document: https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/innovation-better-canada/en/artificial-intelligence-and-data-act-aida-companion-document
  - "Death of AIDA" op-ed (Montreal AI Ethics Institute, Attard-Frost): https://montrealethics.ai/the-death-of-canadas-artificial-intelligence-and-data-act-what-happened-and-whats-next-for-ai-regulation-in-canada/

### 1.2 Consumer Privacy Protection Act (CPPA) — **DEAD** with AIDA

- **Instrument:** Would have replaced PIPEDA (Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act, 2000) with the CPPA + Tribunal Act.
- **Died:** 6 January 2025 (same vehicle as AIDA).
- **Status as of July 2026:** Active PIPEDA still in force. No federal replacement. **Quebec Law 25** is the only comprehensive provincial privacy regime (see §5).
- **Agrifood implications:** Farm data is generally captured under PIPEDA's commercial-activities exemption if held by a "federally regulated works, undertaking, or business" (rare for farms), but most farm data held by vendors falls outside PIPEDA's scope and is governed only by contract. The CPPA would have closed this gap; it died.
- **Sources:** Same as §1.1; additionally https://www.didomi.io/blog/canada-data-privacy-law and the ISED CPPA page https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/innovation-better-canada/en/consumer-privacy-protection-act (now historical).

### 1.3 *AI for All* (Canada's National AI Strategy) — **ACTIVE strategy, NOT legislation**

- **Instrument:** Canada's new national AI strategy, launched by PM Mark Carney on **4 June 2026** in Toronto. Six pillars: Pillar 1 Protecting Canadians; Pillar 2 Empowering Canadians; Pillar 3 Powering adoption (priority sectors: health, energy, transportation, **agriculture**, manufacturing, robotics, government); Pillar 4 Sovereign foundations (compute, cloud, connectivity, data, talent); Pillar 5 Scaling Canadian champions; Pillar 6 Trusted partnerships.
- **Targets:** 250,000 new AI jobs in five years; AI adoption from ~12% to 60% by 2034; up to 90,000 work placements; ~$2B new programme spending (BBC summary; PMO release confirms targets).
- **Agrifood relevance:** **Agriculture is named as a priority sector for adoption.** Croptimistic Technology's soil-mapping product is named in the Minister's Message as the showcase Canadian agrifood AI example. Croptimistic SWAT CAM = 1M acres mapped by 2025 (carried over from existing Canada scan).
- **Binding force:** None. Strategy documents set direction; they do not create obligations. New legislation (privacy, deepfake, online safety, surveillance pricing) is promised but not tabled.
- **Critical assessment:** Mertins-Kirkwood & Pettigrew (CCPA, 5 June 2026) — "AI is never defined in the strategy; generative AI is mentioned only twice; …the strategy specifically blames 'low literacy and low trust' as the biggest barriers…" Charlebois (Agri-Food Analytics Lab, July 2026) — "the problem isn't AI, it's data."

### 1.4 Canadian AI Safety Institute (CAISI) — **ACTIVE**

- **Instrument:** Federal body for evaluating frontier AI models. Launched **November 2024** under Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada.
- **Status as of July 2026:** Confirmed active. AI for All strategy (June 2026) commits to "expanding the capabilities of the Canadian AI Safety Institute to conduct transparent evaluations of AI models." No indication of agrifood-specific scope.
- **Agrifood implications:** None direct. CAISI is a model-evaluation body, not a sector regulator. The European AI Office / US AISI counterpart also does not have direct agrifood jurisdiction.
- **Sources:**

### 1.5 Directive on Automated Decision-Making + Algorithmic Impact Assessment (AIA) — **ACTIVE**

- **Instrument:** Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat instrument in force since **April 2019**, updated periodically. Requires federal departments using automated decision systems to complete an Algorithmic Impact Assessment before production. Applies to federal procurement and service delivery.
- **Agrifood implications:** Limited. The Directive binds federal departments (CFIA, AAFC, Statistics Canada, ESDC for SAWP / Agri-Food Immigration Pilot, etc.). Does *not* apply to private agrifood AI deployment. Worth noting because Statistics Canada (Budget 2025 TechStat program) and CFIA (food safety inspection, traceability) use AIA when they procure or deploy automated decision systems.

### 1.6 Bill C-26 (Critical Infrastructure Cyber Security Act) — **DIED**; revived as Bill C-8 — *not* a new federal Bill C-26 in current Parliament

- **Instrument:** Bill C-26, tabled June 2022, would have introduced the *Critical Infrastructure Cyber Security Act* (CICSA) covering four federally regulated sectors (telecommunications, finance, energy, transport).
- **Status:** Died at prorogation 6 January 2025. Replaced by **Bill C-8** (current Parliament, 45th, 1st Session) — confirmed by Gowling WLG legal analysis, 2025.
- **Agrifood implications:** None direct. The food and agriculture sector is not in scope under Bill C-26 / C-8's "critical infrastructure" definitions. This is a structural absence: agrifood is treated as outside critical-infrastructure protection, which has implications for AI cybersecurity regulation in food processing.

### 1.7 Bill C-244 (Copyright Act amendment for right to repair) — **PASSED, Royal Assent 7 November 2024**

- **Instrument:** Private Member's Bill (MP Wilson-Raybould / Liberal), 44th Parliament. Amends Copyright Act to add section 41.121 making it legal to circumvent technological protection measures (TPMs) for product diagnosis, maintenance, and repair.
- **Agrifood implications:** **Load-bearing** for the on-farm repair rights debate. Canadian ag-equipment owners gain the legal right to bypass TPMs for repair of devices (tractors, combines, software-locked components). Does *not* mandate manufacturer disclosure of parts, tools, manuals, or passwords — that's the missing piece.
- **Cross-border:** US-side FTC-Deere settlement (8 July 2026) and Deere $99M class-action settlement (April 2026) both address the *same* TPM / repair-tool-access pattern. Canadian law now mirrors the substantive copyright carve-out but the operational disclosure layer remains US-driven.

### 1.8 Health Canada / PMRA — RPAS (drone) pesticide application — **NEW, ACTIVE 30 June 2026**

- **Instrument:** **Science Policy Document SPN2026-02** issued by Health Canada / Pest Management Regulatory Agency (PMRA), dated **30 June 2026**, with public consultation PRO2026-01 (23 February 2026, 150+ comments received).
- **Mechanism:** Allows RPAS (Remotely Piloted Aircraft Systems) to apply any pesticide already registered for conventional aerial application, subject to: (a) Transport Canada drone pilot certificate; (b) provincial/territorial compliance; (c) PMRA worker-safety rule that the pilot and mixer/loader must be separate persons (a risk-management simplification, not a permanent rule).
- **Pre-existing constraint (until 30 June 2026):** Pesticide application by drone was only legal for products with explicit "RPAS" or "drone" language on the PMRA label. The new policy lifts this constraint by extending to all products with conventional aerial labels.
- **Agrifood implications:** **Single most consequential federal agrifood-AI-adjacent regulatory move of the cycle.** Brings Canada into alignment with Australia and Japan. Directly unlocks DJI / XAG / custom-built drone deployment for pesticide, fungicide, and foliar application. **Corteva Agriscience is already a named AIVA validation partner** running drone spray trials (existing Canada FCC cycle); the new policy converts those trials into deployable practice.
- **Sources:**
  - Health Canada news release: https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/news/2026/06/government-of-canada-approves-new-technology-to-support-farmers-and-a-more-sustainable-use-of-pest-management-products.html
  - SPN2026-02 full document: https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/consumer-product-safety/reports-publications/pesticides-pest-management/policies-guidelines/science-policy-notes/2026/allowing-pesticide-application-remotely-piloted-aircraft-systems-rpas-drones-products-registered-aerial.html
  - Consultation PRO2026-01: https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/consumer-product-safety/pesticides-pest-management/public/consultations/regulatory-proposals/2026/permitting-pesticide-application-remotely-piloted-aircraft-systems-drones-products-currently-registered-aerial-application.html
  - Industry analysis (Avary Drone): https://avarydrone.com/blogs/learn/agricultural-drones-in-canada-what-you-can-legally-do-in-2026-and-what-s-coming-soon
  - Canadian Agricultural Drone Association (CADA): https://www.canagdrones.ca/regulatory

### 1.9 CFIA — Livestock traceability regulation — **PAUSED then partially advanced**

- **Instrument:** Proposed amendments to Part XV (Traceability) of the Health of Animals Regulations. **Cattle movement reporting requirements** were paused in January 2026 following industry pushback (CCA, Alberta Beef Producers). **June 2026 update:** CFIA confirmed (June 2026) it will move ahead with updated traceability requirements for **goats, sheep, and cervids** but **not cattle at this time**.
- **AI-relevance:** Traceability regulations do not *require* AI, but the proposed framework is AI-compatible: electronic tagging, movement reporting, and integration with supply-chain traceability platforms are the data substrate that downstream ML systems depend on. The cattle pause is a structural delay for AI-ready traceability infrastructure.
- **Sources:**

### 1.10 Cellular agriculture / novel food regulation — **ACTIVE guidance**

- **Instrument:** Health Canada + CFIA joint regulatory guidance (2023–2024) for cellular agriculture products. Most cellular agriculture products regulated as "novel foods" under Division 28 of the Food and Drug Regulations (FDR). Pre-market safety assessment required.
- **AI-relevance:** Indirect. Cellular agriculture uses AI for cell-line optimisation, bioprocess control, and media design. The 2023 guidance is one of the *few* sector-specific regulatory postures Canada has adopted for an AI-adjacent food tech.

### 1.13 Ontario Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act, 2022 — **IN FORCE 1 July 2025**

- **Instrument:** Ontario labour law for ride-share, food delivery, and other gig platform workers. Came into force **1 July 2025**. Establishes minimum wage, notice, and transparency rules for digital platform workers.

### 1.14 Canada–U.S. data adequacy / sovereignty — **STALE BUT CURRENT**

- **Instrument:** EU–Canada data adequacy decision (under GDPR Art. 45) was renewed in **January 2024** explicitly because of C-27 / AIDA / CPPA "efforts." Renewal is contingent on continued modernisation; European Commission stated in the renewal that it would "closely monitor" future efforts.
- **Implication:** Canada is *now* in a softer adequacy posture than at renewal — AIDA/CPPA are dead, no replacement is in force, and the EU Commission could revisit adequacy. This is the structural backstop for "data sovereignty" in Canadian agrifood AI: EU adequacy underwrites the ability of EU-headquartered agrifood vendors and partners (e.g., Sollum is Canadian; Lely, GEA, Big Dutchman are European) to move data freely.
- **Source:** IAPP (Karbaliotis quoted on C-27 and adequacy): https://iapp.org/news/a/what-2026-may-bring-for-canadas-privacy-reform-efforts

## 2. Rural connectivity — the binding constraint, with new negative signals

### 2.1 The headline connectivity figures (Canada)

- **Baseline (Auditor General of Canada Report 2, 2021, 44th Parliament, 1st Session):**
  - National household access at 50/10 Mbps (the federal target): **90.9%** as of 2021.
  - **First Nations reserves: 42.9%.**
  - Rural and remote: 59.5%.
- **Federal targets:** 90% by 2021 ✓; **98% by 2026**; 100% by 2030.
- **Public Accounts committee 2025 (45th Parliament, 1st Session) findings:** Government spent 40% of available connectivity funding by January 2023 ($949M of $2.4B available by FY 2022–23). 10-year spending commitment: $8B total. ISED and CRTC were directed to report progress on coverage mapping by 1 March 2026.

### 2.2 The negative-cycle signals (May–June 2026)

- **UBF will not be renewed.** The Wire Report (paywalled; headline + industry source, 27 May 2026) reports: "Ottawa will not renew its multi-billion dollar funding program designed to connect every household to high-speed internet by 2030, …the Liberal government will rely on satellite offerings to close Canada's digital divide." This is the most consequential negative signal in the connectivity substrate for AI for All.
- **ISED confirms 50/10 Mbps is sufficient** (5 June 2026). The Wire Report: "ISED won't raise high-speed internet standard, says 50/10 Mbps sufficient for Canadian households." This freezes the standard at the 2019 level even as precision agriculture / ML image upload / satellite-link edge inference demand more.
- **CRTC Broadband Fund** (separate from UBF) had a new call for proposals with **$769M available** in 2026 (Telecom Notice of Consultation CRTC 2026-63), accepting proposals until 13 August 2026. $769M is materially smaller than UBF. Decisions pending.
- **Telesat LEO Lightspeed** (Canadian sovereign LEO satellite) remains the named technology for closing the rural gap. Government subsidising Telesat + ISPs to improve broadband in remote areas (Fierce Network, 2026).
- **NWT-specific funding:** ISED announced (May 2026) $13M+ for 869 homes in Northwest Territories — micro-scale relative to the connectivity gap.

### 2.3 Why this matters for AI for All

- AI for All (June 2026) promises a "world-leading public AI supercomputer" and sovereign compute infrastructure. The connectivity substrate *for users* (farmers, rural businesses) is being de-prioritised in parallel. The result is a Canadian AI strategy where AI capability is built *centrally* while the user-side connectivity to deploy / benefit from it remains weak. **Neethirajan's rural-AI critique (§6) gains structural force from this.**

## 5. Quebec-specific regulatory substrate (light in existing scans)

### 5.1 Quebec Law 25 — *in force* (most provisions since Sept 2023, final provisions since 2024)

- **Instrument:** *Act to modernize legislative provisions as regards the protection of personal information*, passed 22 September 2021, in force in phases. **Most provisions: September 2022 / 2023.** Final provisions: 2024. Governs all organisations handling personal information of Quebec residents.
- **Agrifood relevance:** Quebec Law 25 applies to on-farm data held by vendors, agritech platforms, and food retailers operating in Quebec. Provides Quebec residents (and farms) with privacy rights that exceed PIPEDA: data portability, deletion rights, anonymisation, privacy by default, mandatory privacy officer, breach notification to the CAI (Commission d'accès à l'information).
- **Cross-border data transfer:** Law 25 includes transfer-impact provisions; IAPP reports (Feb 2026) that the federal privacy reform discussion is being shaped by Quebec's transfer-impact model.

## 9. Architecture recommendation — should Canada have an EU-style two-sided "funder+regulatory" substrate pair?

### 9.1 The EU two-sided pair, for reference

The EU substrate has (a) a **funder side** (Horizon Europe, Digital Europe, CAP, EIP-AGRI service points) and (b) a **regulatory side** (AI Act, GDPR, Data Act, Machinery Regulation, NIS2). Both sides operate in tandem. The funder side often conditions grants on regulatory compliance (e.g., "data space" interoperability requirements under the European Agricultural Data Space). The regulatory side provides enforceable standards that funder-side deployments must meet.

### 9.2 Canada's substrate, July 2026

**Funder side (relatively strong):**
- **FCC / Farm Credit Canada** — $2B FCC Capital investment commitment + $5B investment coalition; AgExpert platform (25,000 users, 6.5M acres); AIVA Network (May 2026); Root AI. *Federal Crown corporation.*
- **EMILI** — Innovation Farms (10,000 acres in Manitoba); AIVA co-founder; Canadian Agri-Food Data Initiative (CADI). *Industry-led non-profit with federal / provincial backing.*
- **CAAIN** (Canadian Agri-Food Automation and Intelligence Network) — federally supported, 2024–2025 open competition; Smart Farms Program.
- **Protein Industries Canada** — federally supported supercluster.
- **AAFC / AAFC Sustainable Canadian Agricultural Partnership (2023–2028)** — does not name digital agriculture as priority.
- **Pan-Canadian Smart Farm Network** — multi-institution validation network.
- **ISED AI for All** — $2B+ in AI programmes.
- **Provincial:** Mila ($36M Quebec), IVADO (Quebec), Alberta Machine Intelligence Institute (Amii), Vector Institute (Ontario), NRC, AAFC Indigenous Agriculture and Food Systems Initiative ($8.5M, 2018–2023).

**Regulatory side (substantially absent at federal level; fragmented elsewhere):**
- **AIDA** — DEAD.
- **CPPA** — DEAD.
- **Bill C-244 (right to repair)** — PASSED but narrow copyright carve-out; operational disclosure missing.
- **Health Canada SPN2026-02 (drone pesticides)** — ACTIVE, June 2026. **The single most consequential federal agrifood-AI-adjacent regulatory move.**
- **CAISI (AI Safety Institute)** — ACTIVE but no agrifood-specific scope.
- **Directive on Automated Decision-Making + AIA** — ACTIVE; applies to federal departments, not private deployment.
- **Cellular agriculture / novel food guidance** — ACTIVE; not AI-specific.
- **Ontario Bill 194** — ACTIVE; public-sector only.
- **Ontario Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act** — IN FORCE 1 July 2025; gig workers (incl. food delivery); not on-farm.
- **Quebec Law 25** — ACTIVE; comprehensive privacy.
- **Bill C-8 (critical infrastructure cyber)** — IN PARLIAMENT; food/agriculture not in scope.

### 9.3 Diagnosis

The funder side is *comparable in scale and structure* to the EU funder side (although without the explicit "data space" / interoperability requirements). The regulatory side is *fundamentally different in kind*: it is a *sparse, sector-by-sector, largely reactive* posture rather than the EU's *horizontal AI Act + GDPR + Data Act + sector-specific regulations*.

The EU's "funder+regulatory substrate pair" works because the two sides co-evolve and condition each other. Canada's substrate has a strong funder side and a fragmented regulatory side that *cannot condition the funder side* in the same way because the regulatory side lacks horizontal instruments.

### 9.4 Recommendation: **do NOT mirror the EU two-sided pair.** Use an asymmetric three-pronged architecture.

The Canadian scan architecture should distinguish three substrates:

#### Substrate 1: Funder + Convenor (well-populated)

- FCC + FCC Capital + AIVA Network + AgExpert + Root AI
- EMILI + CADI + Innovation Farms
- CAPI (policy institute, federally and industry funded)
- CAAIN, Protein Industries Canada, Pan-Canadian Smart Farm Network
- Mila + IVADO + Vector + Amii (academic)
- AAFC (Sustainable Canadian Agricultural Partnership, Indigenous Agriculture and Food Systems Initiative)
- Statistics Canada TechStat program

*Field-guide treatment:* comparable to the EU funder substrate scan, with emphasis on which deployments condition adoption on regulatory compliance (almost none, currently) and which Indigenous-led organisations are funded (NCIAF; Indigenous Agriculture and Food Systems Initiative projects).

#### Substrate 2: Fragmented Regulatory Substrate (sparse, sector-by-sector)

- Federal (dead): AIDA, CPPA
- Federal (active): CAISI, Directive on ADM / AIA, Bill C-244 (narrow), Health Canada SPN2026-02 (drones)
- Provincial (active): Quebec Law 25, Ontario Bill 194 (public sector), Ontario Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act (gig)
- Federal regulator (sectoral): CFIA (livestock traceability paused / partial), Health Canada (cellular agriculture novel food), Transport Canada (drone pilot certification), PMRA (pesticides)
- Cross-border: EU adequacy status (renewed Jan 2024, contingent on continued modernisation)

*Field-guide treatment:* as a *map of gaps*, not as a regulatory framework. Each instrument has scope, dates, and an explicit "agrifood implication" column. The health-Canada-drone and CFIA-traceability instances are the most directly agrifood-relevant; everything else is horizontal.

#### Substrate 3: Critical-Voice Substrate (substantial but under-coordinated)

- Academic: Neethirajan (Dalhousie, rural + Green AI), Dara (U Guelph, greenhouse + cyber risk), Charlebois (Dalhousie, data-deficit)
- Public-interest: CCPA (Mertins-Kirkwood, Pettigrew), Montreal AI Ethics Institute (Attard-Frost), Schwartz Reisman Institute (U Toronto)
- Labour: UFCW Canada (food processing, gig workers), CLC submission on AIDA
- Producer: NFU, CFA, AWA
- Indigenous-led: NCIAF, FNIGC / OCAP, CARE Principles authors, IEEE 2890 working group, Collaboratory for Indigenous Data Governance
- Co-operative alternative: CCSC (U Saskatchewan), Murray Fulton

*Field-guide treatment:* as a *plural critical landscape*, distinguishing rural-policy (Neethirajan), data-governance (Charlebois / CCSC), labour (UFCW / CLC), producer (NFU / CFA), Indigenous data sovereignty (FNIGC / CARE / NCIAF), and food sovereignty (the *absent* Canadian equivalent of IPES-Food). This matches the regional scan's existing taxonomy proposal to distinguish `critical-voice` sub-tags.

### 9.5 Practical scan-architecture implication

For the Canada regional scan, treat the three substrates as **three separate content streams** with their own freshness dates:

| Substrate | Refresh cadence | Anchor source |
|---|---|---|
| Funder + Convenor | Annual | FCC + EMILI + CAPI primary docs |
| Fragmented Regulatory | Quarterly (high volatility) | parl.ca LEGISinfo + ISED + Health Canada + CRTC |
| Critical Voice | Quarterly (new academic + labour pieces frequent) | Policy Options + Frontiers + Agri-Food Analytics Lab + UFCW + NFU |

The current scans (FCC cycle, Quebec cycle, regional scan) are *Substrate 1* dominant. The scout findings above (this document) are *Substrate 2 + Substrate 3* material that should inform a dedicated **Canada constraint/critical scan** in the next cycle.

### 9.6 One concrete integration proposal for the field guide

Add to the next Canada regional cycle a `Canada Regulatory Substrate` unit that mirrors the EU regulatory-substrate unit pattern but is *honest about its sparseness*. Pair it with the existing funder-substrate treatment. The pairing is asymmetric and should be labelled as such — "Canada Funder + Fragmented Regulatory" rather than "Canada Funder + Regulatory." This naming alone signals to the reader that Canada is structurally different from the EU.
